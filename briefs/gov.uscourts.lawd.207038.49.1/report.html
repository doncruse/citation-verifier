p. 3 — United States v. McRae, 593 F.2d 700, 707 (5th Cir. 1979) — Reworded -- not a verbatim quote
What the brief claims:

The Fifth Circuit has emphasized that Rule 403 grants trial courts "wide discretion" to exclude evidence where the risk of prejudice outweighs probative value. United States v. McRae, 593 F.2d 700, 707 (5th Cir. 1979).

Actual language in opinion:

The remaining two claimed errors involve evidentiary rulings by the district court. As to rulings of this sort, it is a commonplace that the trial court enjoys a wide discretion, one which we do not disturb except for abuse.

The quoted phrase "wide discretion" does appear verbatim on page 707 of the McRae opinion, but the brief's framing misattributes its scope. McRae does not say that Rule 403 itself grants trial courts "wide discretion"; rather, the opinion uses that phrase as a general observation about evidentiary rulings by district courts ("the trial court enjoys a wide discretion, one which we do not disturb except for abuse"). The opinion then discusses Rule 403 in the specific context of challenged photographs of the murder victim.

McRae does support the broader proposition that trial courts have wide discretion over Rule 403 determinations -- the opinion describes Rule 403's function, noting that "it is only unfair prejudice, substantially outweighing probative value, which permits exclusion of relevant matter under Rule 403" and that application of Rule 403 "must be cautious and sparing." The opinion's characterization of the trial court's discretion supports the brief's general proposition, though the direct quote "wide discretion" is a generic statement about evidentiary rulings rather than a Rule-403-specific holding. The minor stretch in attributing the phrase specifically to Rule 403 is a light rewording rather than a material misrepresentation of the case.

What the brief claims:

As one court aptly explained, the human body is fundamentally different from inanimate evidence: "Gilliam's body is fundamentally different from a vehicle or other inanimate piece of evidence… plaintiffs are free to determine when they should undergo medical treatment deemed necessary by their physicians." Gilliam v. Uni Holdings, LLC, 201 A.D.3d 83, 88–89 (1st Dep't 2021). The court emphasized that "a plaintiff's decision to undergo surgery does not constitute spoliation of evidence."

Actual language in opinion:

The state of one's body is fundamentally different from inanimate evidence, and medical treatment, including surgery, is entirely distinct from the destruction of documents or tangible evidence which spoliation sanctions attempt to ameliorate. ... Plaintiffs must be free to determine when to undergo medical treatments based on personal factors such as doctor's advice and their specific pain and discomfort level. ... Thus, plaintiff's pre-ME spine surgery did not result in the spoliation of evidence.

The underlying proposition is fully supported by Gilliam—the First Department held that the condition of one's body is not subject to spoliation analysis and that plaintiffs must be free to make medical decisions. However, neither of the passages presented in quotation marks appears verbatim in the opinion. The actual language is 'The state of one's body is fundamentally different from inanimate evidence' (not 'Gilliam's body is fundamentally different from a vehicle or other inanimate piece of evidence'), and 'Plaintiffs must be free to determine when to undergo medical treatments based on personal factors such as doctor's advice and their specific pain and discomfort level' (not 'plaintiffs are free to determine when they should undergo medical treatment deemed necessary by their physicians'). The second quoted sentence—'a plaintiff's decision to undergo surgery does not constitute spoliation of evidence'—is also not verbatim; the opinion states 'plaintiff's pre-ME spine surgery did not result in the spoliation of evidence.' Because the substantive proposition is supported but the quotation marks misrepresent the opinion's actual wording, this is reworded/paraphrased language presented as direct quotation.

Therefore, King must show that ICR acted in "bad faith" to establish that it was entitled to an adverse inference.

King does require a showing of bad faith for an adverse spoliation inference, so the brief's underlying proposition is supported. p. 9 — Michelson v. United States, 335 U.S. 469, 475-76 (1948) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

The Supreme Court has long recognized the prejudicial impact of arrest evidence where no conviction exists. Michelson v. United States, 335 U.S. 469, 475–76 (1948).

Actual language in opinion:

Arrest without more does not, in law any more than in reason, impeach the integrity or impair the credibility of a witness. It happens to the innocent as well as the guilty. Only a conviction, therefore, may be inquired about to undermine the trustworthiness of a witness. Arrest without more may nevertheless impair or cloud one's reputation.

Michelson does recognize the prejudicial impact of arrest evidence, but the brief's pinpoint cite (pp. 475–76) is off. Pages 475–76 discuss the common-law framework for character evidence generally—explaining that the overriding policy of excluding such evidence is that 'its disallowance tends to prevent confusion of issues, unfair surprise and undue prejudice'—without focusing on arrests specifically. The arrest-specific discussion (including Justice Jackson's statement that 'Arrest without more does not, in law any more than in reason, impeach the integrity') appears later in the opinion at page 482. The proposition has support in Michelson overall, but the cited pages do not establish the specific point about arrest evidence.

p. 10 — United States v. McRae, 593 F.2d 700, 707 (5th Cir. 1979) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Under Rule 403, this evidence must be excluded because any minimal probative value is substantially outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues, and waste of time. United States v. McRae, 593 F.2d 700, 707 (5th Cir. 1979).

Actual language in opinion:

Relevant evidence is inherently prejudicial; but it is only unfair prejudice, substantially outweighing probative value, which permits exclusion of relevant matter under Rule 403. Unless trials are to be conducted on scenarios, on unreal facts tailored and sanitized for the occasion, the application of Rule 403 must be cautious and sparing. Its major function is limited to excluding matter of scant or cumulative probative force, dragged in by the heels for the sake of its prejudicial effect.

The McRae opinion discusses Rule 403 at page 707, but the framing there is substantially less favorable to exclusion than the brief suggests. McRae emphasizes that Rule 403 "must be cautious and sparing" and that its "major function is limited to excluding matter of scant or cumulative probative force, dragged in by the heels for the sake of its prejudicial effect." The opinion stresses that only unfair prejudice -- "substantially outweighing" probative value -- justifies exclusion of relevant evidence.

In McRae itself, the Fifth Circuit refused to exclude gruesome photographs of the deceased murder victim, holding that the trial court did not abuse its discretion. The court went out of its way to warn against using Rule 403 "to 'even out' the weight of the evidence, to mitigate a crime, or to make a contest where there is little or none." McRae is thus better authority for admitting probative evidence despite prejudicial effect than for excluding it.

While the cited opinion does recite the Rule 403 standard, citing McRae as authority requiring that evidence "must be excluded" understates the case's pro-admission orientation. McRae supports the general legal framework but does not stand for a rule that evidence of marginal probative value must be excluded.

p. 11 — United States v. Abel, 469 U.S. 45, 52 (1984) — Inverts the holding
What the brief claims:

The Supreme Court has addressed the proper scope of bias evidence, emphasizing that such evidence must demonstrate actual bias affecting the witness's testimony. United States v. Abel, 469 U.S. 45, 52 (1984).

Actual language in opinion:

Proof of bias is almost always relevant because the jury, as finder of fact and weigher of credibility, has historically been entitled to assess all evidence which might bear on the accuracy and truth of a witness' testimony. ... A witness' and a party's common membership in an organization, even without proof that the witness or party has personally adopted its tenets, is certainly probative of bias.

Abel holds the opposite of what the brief suggests. At page 52, the Supreme Court emphasized the broad admissibility of bias evidence—'Proof of bias is almost always relevant'—and specifically held that common membership in an organization 'even without proof that the witness or party has personally adopted its tenets, is certainly probative of bias.' The Court explicitly rejected any requirement that the proponent demonstrate 'actual bias'; it was enough that membership supported an inference of potential bias. The brief inverts this holding by suggesting Abel imposes a heightened 'actual bias' requirement, when Abel in fact stands for broad and permissive admission of bias evidence on a relevance theory under Rules 401 and 403.
