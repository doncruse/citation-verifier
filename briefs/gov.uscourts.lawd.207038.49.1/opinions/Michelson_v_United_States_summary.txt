CASE SUMMARY:
Michelson was convicted of bribing a federal revenue agent in 1947. The primary issue on appeal was whether the trial court properly allowed the prosecution to cross-examine the defendant's character witnesses about an arrest for receiving stolen goods from 1920, where no conviction had occurred.

KEY HOLDINGS:
- Character witnesses may be cross-examined about arrests, not just convictions
- Arrest without conviction may impair or cloud one's reputation in the community
- Trial courts have discretion to limit character evidence questioning

TOPICS FOUND:
- Proposition 15: "Arrest without more does not, in law any more than in reason, impeach the integrity or impair the credibility of a witness. It happens to the innocent as well as the guilty... Arrest without more may nevertheless impair or cloud one's reputation. False arrest may do that." (Page 482-483). The Court also recognized the "prejudicial impact" by noting the jury "almost surely cannot comprehend the judge's limiting instruction" (Page 484-485).
