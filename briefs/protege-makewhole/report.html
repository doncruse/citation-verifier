p. general-rule — Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. v. Hertz Corp. (In re Hertz Corp.), 637 B.R. 781 (2021) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

claims for unmatured interest are disallowed in bankruptcy. Courts have often treated make-whole premiums as the economic equivalent of unmatured interest, leading to their disallowance in many cases. Reorganized Debtors v. Hertz Corp. (In re Hertz Corp.), 2024 U.S. App. LEXIS 34433 (2024), Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. v. Hertz Corp. (In re Hertz Corp.), 637 B.R. 781 (2021).

Actual language in opinion:

Calling a make-whole a contract right or a liquidated damages provision does not answer the question of whether it is unmatured interest. ... Instead, the Court concludes that the determination of whether the redemption premium that Wells Fargo seeks in this case is, in fact, the economic equivalent of unmatured interest is not a legal question, but is instead a factual one ... the Court concludes that Wells Fargo may be able to present evidence that the redemption premium in the 2026/2028 Senior Notes is not, in fact, the economic equivalent of unmatured interest due under those Senior Notes.

Judge Walrath's 2021 opinion supports the general proposition only partially. She acknowledged and surveyed case law treating make-whole premiums as economic equivalents of unmatured interest subject to disallowance under Section 502(b)(2), and held that courts 'look to the economic substance of the transaction to determine what counts as interest.' However, at the motion-to-dismiss stage she did NOT disallow the make-whole: she denied Hertz's motion as to the 2026/2028 Notes, holding that whether the premium is the economic equivalent of unmatured interest is a fact question requiring discovery. So the opinion supports the general doctrinal point that make-wholes can be and sometimes are disallowed as unmatured interest, but the 637 B.R. 781 ruling itself is not an example of disallowance at this stage (disallowance came later on summary judgment, which the 3d Cir affirmed in 2024). Citing this opinion for the proposition is defensible but slightly overstates what this particular ruling did.

p. ny-law-sdny — Apollo Global Mgmt., LLC v. Bokf, NA (In re MPM Silicones, L.L.C.), 874 F.3d 787 (2017) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

New York law generally enforces make-whole premiums as liquidated damages if they are reasonable and not a penalty. However, in bankruptcy, SDNY courts have consistently required clear and unambiguous language in the governing agreements to enforce make-whole premiums after acceleration. For instance, in Momentive, the SDNY Bankruptcy Court denied enforcement of a make-whole premium, emphasizing that the indenture must explicitly provide for such payment following acceleration. Apollo Global Mgmt., LLC v. Bokf, NA (In re MPM Silicones, L.L.C.), 874 F.3d 787 (2017), Del. Trust Co. v. Energy Future Intermediate Holding Co. LLC (In re Energy Future Holdings Corp.), 842 F.3d 247 (2016) [...]

Actual language in opinion:

The bankruptcy court concluded that the Senior-Lien Notes holders were not entitled to the premium. It reasoned that under the 2012 Indentures the make-whole premium would be due only in the case of an 'optional redemption' and not in the case of an acceleration brought about by a bankruptcy filing. 2014 WL 4436335, at *11-*15. The district court agreed. 531 B.R. at 335-38. We too agree.

The brief's substantive description of the Momentive holding is accurate: the SDNY Bankruptcy Court (Judge Drain) denied enforcement of the make-whole because the 2012 Indentures' Optional Redemption Clauses did not trigger upon bankruptcy-caused automatic acceleration, and the court required indenture language that specifically provided for the make-whole after acceleration. The Second Circuit opinion cited (874 F.3d 787) recounts and affirms exactly that reasoning. The minor gap is that 874 F.3d 787 is the circuit affirmance, not the bankruptcy court opinion the brief invokes; a more precise cite would be to 2014 WL 4436335 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y.) or 531 B.R. 321 (the district court affirmance). Because the Second Circuit opinion expressly describes and endorses the bankruptcy court's reasoning, citing it is a defensible shortcut rather than a misattribution.

p. ny-law-sdny — Del. Trust Co. v. Energy Future Intermediate Holding Co. LLC (In re Energy Future Holdings Corp.), 842 F.3d 247 (2016) — Inverts the holding
What the brief claims:

New York law generally enforces make-whole premiums as liquidated damages if they are reasonable and not a penalty. However, in bankruptcy, SDNY courts have consistently required clear and unambiguous language in the governing agreements to enforce make-whole premiums after acceleration. For instance, in Momentive, the SDNY Bankruptcy Court denied enforcement of a make-whole premium, emphasizing that the indenture must explicitly provide for such payment following acceleration. [...] Del. Trust Co. v. Energy Future Intermediate Holding Co. LLC (In re Energy Future Holdings Corp.), 842 F.3d 247 (2016) [...]

Actual language in opinion:

We believe, however, the result in Momentive conflicts with that indenture's text and fails to honor the parties' bargain. For these and additional reasons discussed below, we find it unpersuasive. [...] Recently, however, bankruptcy courts, including the Bankruptcy Court here, have stretched Northwestern beyond its language and applied its clear-statement rule to yield-protection payments not styled as prepayment premiums.

EFH is cited for the proposition that Momentive's clear-and-unambiguous-language rule is the governing approach, but the Third Circuit in EFH does the opposite: Judge Ambro expressly rejects Momentive as 'unpersuasive' and criticizes bankruptcy courts for 'stretch[ing] Northwestern beyond its language' by extending the clear-statement rule to make-wholes that are not styled as prepayment premiums. EFH holds that under New York law (per NML Capital), contract terms 'not necessarily impacted by acceleration . . . [do not] automatically cease to be enforceable after acceleration,' so the noteholders were entitled to the make-whole despite the absence of explicit post-acceleration language. Citing EFH for the Momentive rule therefore inverts the Third Circuit's actual holding. (Also note the proposition attributes the rule to 'SDNY courts,' but EFH is a Third Circuit opinion reviewing a Delaware bankruptcy case.)

p. exception-clear-language — Paloian v. LaSalle Bank N.A (In re Doctors Hosp. of Hyde Park, Inc.), 508 B.R. 697 (2014) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Clear and Unambiguous Language: If the indenture explicitly states that the make-whole premium is payable even after acceleration, courts may enforce it. For example, in In re Madison 92nd St. Associates LLC, a prepayment premium was upheld under New York law because the agreement contained clear language requiring payment even after default and acceleration. Paloian v. LaSalle Bank N.A (In re Doctors Hosp. of Hyde Park, Inc.), 508 B.R. 697 (2014).

Actual language in opinion:

Prepayment premiums are generally enforceable under the New York common law 'rule of perfect tender in time.' [...] Where . . . a clear and unambiguous clause requires the payment of the prepayment premium even after default and acceleration, the clause will be analyzed as a liquidated damages clause. In re Madison 92nd St. Associates LLC, 472 B.R. 189, 195-96 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2012).

Paloian does quote the exact Madison 92nd Street passage the brief paraphrases, so using Paloian as a pincite for the Madison 92nd Street proposition is defensible as an 'example' citation. However, Paloian itself does not uphold a prepayment premium under Madison 92nd Street's 'clear and unambiguous' exception -- Judge Schmetterer instead holds the Yield Maintenance Premium is unmatured interest under Section 502(b)(2) and denies summary judgment only because of a factual dispute about whether the creditor was oversecured. Paloian also does not involve post-acceleration enforcement in Madison's sense; acceleration occurred post-petition when LaSalle filed foreclosure. So Paloian supports the background rule that clear language allowing post-acceleration premiums is enforceable under New York law, but readers could be misled to think Paloian itself enforced such a premium.

p. exception-solvent-debtor — Ultra Petroleum Corp. v. Ad Hoc Comm., 943 F.3d 758 (2019) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Solvent Debtor Exception: If the debtor is solvent, courts may apply the 'solvent-debtor exception' to honor contractual obligations, including make-whole premiums, despite the general disallowance of unmatured interest under (11 USCS Section 502.b)(2). [...] Ultra Petroleum Corp. v. Ad Hoc Comm., 943 F.3d 758 (2019).

Actual language in opinion:

Our review of the record reveals no reason why the solvent-debtor exception could not apply. As other circuits have recognized, 'absent compelling equitable considerations, when a debtor is solvent, it is the role of the bankruptcy court to enforce the creditors' contractual rights.' [...] But 'mindful that we are a court of review, not of first view,' we will not make the choice ourselves or weigh the equities on our own. [...] Accordingly, the bankruptcy court should consider the Make-Whole Amount, the appropriate post-petition interest rate, and the applicability of the solvent-debtor exception on remand.

The 2019 Ultra Petroleum opinion does raise the solvent-debtor exception and expressly leaves the door open to its application, stating it sees 'no reason why the solvent-debtor exception could not apply' and citing Dow Corning and Chicago, Milwaukee for the principle that courts should enforce creditors' contractual rights when the debtor is solvent. That broadly supports the brief's hedged 'courts may apply' framing. However, the Fifth Circuit did not hold that the exception applies; it remanded, explicitly declining to 'make the choice ourselves or weigh the equities,' and cautioned that any equitable power must be exercised 'within the confines of the Bankruptcy Code' (quoting Law v. Siegel). The brief's proposition is directionally accurate but overstates Ultra Petroleum as authority for applying the exception when it is really authority only for preserving the question on remand.
