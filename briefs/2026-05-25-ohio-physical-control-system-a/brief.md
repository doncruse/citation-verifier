Physical Control Under Ohio Law: Two Scenarios

Ohio criminalizes being in "physical control" of a vehicle while impaired under ORC 4511.194, which defines physical control as "being in the driver's position of the front seat of a vehicle... and having possession of the vehicle's... ignition key or other ignition device." Both elements — driver's seat and possession of the keys — must be present. *Ohio Revised Code*

The doctrine's foundation is *Cincinnati v. Kelley*, 47 Ohio St. 2d 94 (1976), where the Ohio Supreme Court articulated the test before the legislature codified it: a person is in physical control when they are "in the driver's seat of a vehicle, behind the steering wheel, in possession of the ignition key, and in such condition that he is physically capable of starting the engine and causing the vehicle to move." *Kelley* remains the touchstone for interpreting the statute's elements. *Duidefenseohio*

**Scenario one — passenger seat, keys in the glovebox.** No physical control. The seat element fails on the face of the statute. *Kelley* and ORC 4511.194 both require the driver's position, and a passenger doesn't occupy it. The keys' location is immaterial because the analysis stops at the first element. (A separate "operation" theory under ORC 4511.19 could still apply if the State proved the person drove to that spot before climbing over — see *State v. Mackie*, 128 Ohio App. 3d 167 (1st Dist. 1998), discussing the contours of "operation" — but that's a different offense built on different proof.)

**Scenario two — driver's seat, keys in the glovebox.** Likely physical control. The seat element is satisfied. The contested question is whether keys in the glovebox constitute "possession" of the ignition key. Ohio courts read possession broadly and do not require the key to be in the ignition or on the defendant's person. As one practitioner summary puts it, "having the keys within reach will satisfy the definition of having 'physical control,'" and the statute carries no safe-harbor for keys kept "nearby." A glovebox within arm's length of a driver-seated defendant falls comfortably within that constructive-possession reading — the defendant is, in *Kelley*'s words, "physically capable of starting the engine." *Charles M Rowland II*

The defense argument worth flagging: "possession" should mean more than proximity, and a defendant who deliberately stowed keys out of the ignition has done the opposite of preparing to drive. Ohio appellate courts have not adopted that narrower reading, and the statute's evident purpose — preventing impaired persons from being one motion away from driving — cuts against it.

**Bottom line.** Scenario one is not physical control. Scenario two probably is, though the glovebox detail gives a defense lawyer something to argue. (General information, not legal advice — facts matter and an attorney should evaluate any actual case.)
