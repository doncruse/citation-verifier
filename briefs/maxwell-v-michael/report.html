p. 2 — Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.A., 534 U.S. 506, 514 (2002) — Case on unrelated subject
Quoted in brief:
“A defendant may not deny allegations that are supported by the defendant's own prior admissions or undisputed evidence.”

Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.A. is a Supreme Court employment-discrimination case about whether a Title VII/ADEA complaint must plead a prima facie case of discrimination under the McDonnell Douglas framework. It does not address Rule 8(b)(6) denials, answer pleading, or the effect of prior admissions or documentary evidence on denials. The decision concerns Rule 8(a)(2)'s 'short and plain statement' standard for complaints, holding that a plaintiff need not allege specific facts establishing each McDonnell Douglas element. Nothing in the opinion discusses defendants' obligations when framing denials or the treatment of admissions in prior pleadings. The quoted sentence does not appear in the opinion, and the pinpoint page (514) falls within the Court's discussion of complaint pleading standards, not answer requirements.

David v. Caterpillar, Inc. is a Seventh Circuit Title VII sex-discrimination and retaliation case reviewing a jury verdict, evidentiary rulings under Rules 26 and 37, and damages awards following the denial of a promotion in an employer's security department. It does not address Rule 8(b) answer-pleading standards or a party's ability to claim lack of knowledge about facts within its own control. The opinion's discussion on the cited pinpoint page concerns discovery disclosure obligations and trial evidentiary issues, not the substantive content of denials in an answer. The quoted sentence does not appear anywhere in the opinion and represents a proposition foreign to the court's analysis.

p. 2 — Sojka v. Bovis Lend Lease, Inc., 686 F.3d 394, 398-99 (7th Cir. 2012) — Case on unrelated subject
Quoted in brief:
“A party may not deny on information and belief matters within its personal knowledge or control.”

Sojka v. It does not address Rule 8(b) answer pleadings or denials on information and belief. The cited pages (398-99) discuss the interaction between Rule 56 and Local Rule 56.1, not any rule governing the sufficiency of denials. The quoted proposition does not appear in the opinion and is unrelated to the court's summary-judgment analysis.

p. 2 — United States v. Kasuboski, 834 F.2d 1345, 1350 (7th Cir. 1987) — Overstated -- case partially supports
Quoted in brief:
“Judicial admissions in pleadings are binding and may not be controverted at trial absent leave to amend.”
Actual language in opinion:

Admissions made under Rule 36, even default admissions, can serve as the factual predicate for summary judgment. ... Rule 36(b) provides that a matter admitted is 'conclusively established.' ... [A] party cannot attack issues of fact established in admissions by resisting a motion for summary judgment. ... Affidavits and depositions entered in opposition to summary judgment that attempt to establish issues of fact cannot refute default admissions.

Kasuboski does support the general principle that admissions are binding and cannot be controverted absent a proper motion to withdraw them -- but the admissions it discusses are Rule 36 requests-for-admission, deemed admitted by the defendant's failure to respond within 30 days, not judicial admissions contained in pleadings under Rule 8. The opinion explains that 'the proper procedural vehicle through which to attempt to withdraw admissions made in these circumstances is a motion under Rule 36(b) to withdraw admissions,' and the court's reasoning is grounded in Rule 36(b)'s 'conclusively established' language and Rule 56(c)'s reference to 'admissions on file.'

The brief's quoted proposition -- that 'judicial admissions in pleadings are binding and may not be controverted at trial absent leave to amend' -- states a cognate doctrine but is not the holding or language of Kasuboski. Courts often recognize both Rule 36 admissions and judicial admissions in pleadings as binding, but Kasuboski is a Rule 36 case and does not discuss pleading admissions or leave to amend under Rule 15. The brief therefore overstates the case's reach by attributing to it a proposition about judicial admissions in pleadings that the court did not address.

The quoted sentence does not appear verbatim in the opinion, and the underlying principle, while analogous, is applied to a different procedural mechanism.

Quoted in brief:
“When the documentary evidence makes clear that the allegations in the complaint could not have occurred as alleged, those allegations must be disregarded.”

Anderson v. Branen is a Bivens excessive-force case about a physical altercation between DEA agents and two civilians outside a Manhattan parking garage, holding that law enforcement officials have an affirmative duty to intercede when fellow officers violate a citizen's constitutional rights in their presence. It does not address timestamps, documentary evidence, the scienter element of a false statement, or the distinction between knowing falsehoods and mistaken or negligent ones. The cite supplies no support for the proposition that timestamp-impossible statements are necessarily knowing rather than negligent.

p. 4 — United States v. McMurtrey, 704 F.3d 502, 508 (7th Cir. 2013) — Reworded -- not a verbatim quote
Quoted in brief:
“obvious reasons to doubt the truth of what he or she is asserting.”
Actual language in opinion:

The circumstantial evidence in McMurtrey's Franks motion permitted a reasonable inference of falsity because it provided "obvious reasons to doubt the veracity" of the allegations. See United States v. Whitley, 249 F.3d 614, 621 (7th Cir.2001) ... quoting United States v. Williams, 737 F.2d 594, 602 (7th Cir.1984).

McMurtrey does articulate the Franks reckless-disregard standard using the 'obvious reasons to doubt' formulation, but the opinion (quoting Whitley/Williams) says 'obvious reasons to doubt the veracity of the allegations,' not 'obvious reasons to doubt the truth of what he or she is asserting.' The brief presents this as a direct quote ('have evidentiary support.' style quotation), but the operative phrase has been reworded. The substance of the proposition survives -- the Seventh Circuit does apply this standard for reckless disregard under Franks -- but the quoted language is not verbatim and should not appear in quotation marks.

p. 9 — Hafer v. Melo, 502 U.S. 21, 30-31 (1991) — Paraphrase presented as direct quote
Quoted in brief:
“Individual-capacity suits for damages under § 1983 are not barred by the Eleventh Amendment.”
Actual language in opinion:

We hold that state officials, sued in their individual capacities, are “persons” within the meaning of § 1983. The Eleventh Amendment does not bar such suits, nor are state officers absolutely immune from personal liability under § 1983 solely by virtue of the “official” nature of their acts.

Hafer holds that individual-capacity suits for damages under § 1983 are not barred by the Eleventh Amendment, so the brief's proposition is substantively correct. However, the brief encloses its articulation of that holding in quotation marks, and that exact sentence does not appear in the opinion. The Court's actual formulation is broader and phrased differently (quoted above). Presenting a paraphrase as a direct quote misleads the reader about what the Court said, even when the underlying point is accurate.

p. 10 — Wilson v. Isaacs, 929 N.E.2d 200, 203-04 (Ind. 2010) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Intentional torts such as false arrest and malicious prosecution fall outside ITCA protection
Actual language in opinion:

(8) The ... enforcement of ... a law (including rules and regulations), unless the act of enforcement constitutes false arrest or false imprisonment.

Wilson quotes the ITCA's law-enforcement immunity provision and notes the statutory carve-out 'unless the act of enforcement constitutes false arrest or false imprisonment.' The court's holding addresses excessive force, concluding that 'a law enforcement officer's use of force in excess of the reasonable force authorized by statute is not shielded from liability' under the ITCA. Nothing in Wilson speaks to malicious prosecution. The brief's proposition that 'intentional torts such as false arrest and malicious prosecution fall outside ITCA protection' overstates the case: Wilson supports the false-arrest/false-imprisonment point (which comes straight from the statute), but it does not support the malicious-prosecution extension.

p. 16 — Beauchamp v. City of Noblesville, 320 F.3d 733, 743-44 (7th Cir. 2003) — Inverts the holding
Quoted in brief:
“Probable cause dissipates when officers receive exculpatory evidence yet fail to act.”
Actual language in opinion:

Once an officer has established probable cause on every element of a crime, he need not continue investigating to test the suspect’s claim of innocence.

Beauchamp squarely rejects the proposition the brief attributes to it. The Seventh Circuit held that 'once an officer learns sufficient trustworthy information establishing probable cause, he is entitled to rely on what he knows in pursuing charges or an arrest, and is under no further duty to investigate,' and further that 'once an officer has established probable cause on every element of a crime, he need not continue investigating to test the suspect's claim of innocence.' The court affirmed summary judgment for the detectives on qualified-immunity and false-arrest grounds, concluding that even Beauchamp's alibi and the inconsistencies in the complainant's account did not defeat probable cause. The brief's proposition -- that probable cause dissipates when officers receive exculpatory evidence yet fail to act -- is the losing argument in Beauchamp, not its holding. The quoted sentence also does not appear in the opinion.

Methodology & Limitations

  • Quote verification is near-deterministic but limited by the quality of CourtListener's text. Minor discrepancies may reflect OCR artifacts in the source database rather than errors in the brief.
  • Propositional support assessment is an AI judgment. It reflects Claude's reading of the opinion text and should be verified by counsel. Reasonable lawyers may disagree about whether a case "supports" a given proposition.
  • This report checks whether cited cases say what the brief claims they say. It does not assess the overall legal merit of the brief's arguments or whether the cited cases are the best authorities available.
  • Cases marked "unable to verify" should be checked through other sources (Westlaw, Lexis, etc.).
