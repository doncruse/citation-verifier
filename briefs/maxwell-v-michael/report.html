p. 2 — Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.A., 534 U.S. 506, 514 (2002) — Not supported by cited case
Quoted in brief:
“A defendant may not deny allegations that are supported by the defendant's own prior admissions or undisputed evidence.”
Actual language in opinion:

This ease presents the question whether a complaint in an employment discrimination lawsuit must contain specific facts establishing a prima facie case of discrimination under the framework set forth by this Court in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U. S. 792 (1973). We hold that an employment discrimination complaint need not include such facts and instead must contain only 'a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief.' Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 8(a)(2).

Swierkiewicz addresses the pleading standard for a plaintiff's complaint under Rule 8(a)(2) in employment discrimination cases, holding that a plaintiff need not plead a prima facie McDonnell Douglas case to survive a motion to dismiss. It has nothing to do with Rule 8(b)(6) or the sufficiency of a defendant's denials in an answer, and the opinion never discusses whether a party may deny allegations established by documentary evidence or prior admissions.

The brief's quoted sentence -- 'A defendant may not deny allegations that are supported by the defendant's own prior admissions or undisputed evidence' -- does not appear in the opinion in any form and is not a holding or rationale of the case. The citation is off-topic: Swierkiewicz cannot support a Rule 8(b)(6) denial-sufficiency proposition.

The cited pinpoint (page 514) contains the Court's discussion of Rule 8(a)'s notice-pleading regime and the liberal construction of pleadings, not anything about defensive denials.

p. 2 — United States v. Kasuboski, 834 F.2d 1345, 1350 (7th Cir. 1987) — Overstated -- case partially supports
Quoted in brief:
“Judicial admissions in pleadings are binding and may not be controverted at trial absent leave to amend.”
Actual language in opinion:

Admissions made under Rule 36, even default admissions, can serve as the factual predicate for summary judgment. ... Rule 36(b) provides that a matter admitted is 'conclusively established.' ... [A] party cannot attack issues of fact established in admissions by resisting a motion for summary judgment. ... Affidavits and depositions entered in opposition to summary judgment that attempt to establish issues of fact cannot refute default admissions.

Kasuboski does support the general principle that admissions are binding and cannot be controverted absent a proper motion to withdraw them -- but the admissions it discusses are Rule 36 requests-for-admission, deemed admitted by the defendant's failure to respond within 30 days, not judicial admissions contained in pleadings under Rule 8. The opinion explains that 'the proper procedural vehicle through which to attempt to withdraw admissions made in these circumstances is a motion under Rule 36(b) to withdraw admissions,' and the court's reasoning is grounded in Rule 36(b)'s 'conclusively established' language and Rule 56(c)'s reference to 'admissions on file.'

The brief's quoted proposition -- that 'judicial admissions in pleadings are binding and may not be controverted at trial absent leave to amend' -- states a cognate doctrine but is not the holding or language of Kasuboski. Courts often recognize both Rule 36 admissions and judicial admissions in pleadings as binding, but Kasuboski is a Rule 36 case and does not discuss pleading admissions or leave to amend under Rule 15. The brief therefore overstates the case's reach by attributing to it a proposition about judicial admissions in pleadings that the court did not address.

The quoted sentence does not appear verbatim in the opinion, and the underlying principle, while analogous, is applied to a different procedural mechanism.

p. 16 — Beauchamp v. City of Noblesville, 320 F.3d 733, 743-44 (7th Cir. 2003) — Inverts the holding
Quoted in brief:
“Probable cause dissipates when officers receive exculpatory evidence yet fail to act.”
Actual language in opinion:

Once an officer has established probable cause on every element of a crime, he need not continue investigating to test the suspect’s claim of innocence.

Beauchamp squarely rejects the proposition the brief attributes to it. The Seventh Circuit held that 'once an officer learns sufficient trustworthy information establishing probable cause, he is entitled to rely on what he knows in pursuing charges or an arrest, and is under no further duty to investigate,' and further that 'once an officer has established probable cause on every element of a crime, he need not continue investigating to test the suspect's claim of innocence.' The court affirmed summary judgment for the detectives on qualified-immunity and false-arrest grounds, concluding that even Beauchamp's alibi and the inconsistencies in the complainant's account did not defeat probable cause. The brief's proposition -- that probable cause dissipates when officers receive exculpatory evidence yet fail to act -- is the losing argument in Beauchamp, not its holding. The quoted sentence also does not appear in the opinion.

Methodology & Limitations

  • Quote verification is near-deterministic but limited by the quality of CourtListener's text. Minor discrepancies may reflect OCR artifacts in the source database rather than errors in the brief.
  • Propositional support assessment is an AI judgment. It reflects Claude's reading of the opinion text and should be verified by counsel. Reasonable lawyers may disagree about whether a case "supports" a given proposition.
  • This report checks whether cited cases say what the brief claims they say. It does not assess the overall legal merit of the brief's arguments or whether the cited cases are the best authorities available.
  • Cases marked "unable to verify" should be checked through other sources (Westlaw, Lexis, etc.).
