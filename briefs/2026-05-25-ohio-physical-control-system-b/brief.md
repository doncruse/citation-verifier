# Ohio R.C. 4511.194 "Physical Control" — Doctrinal Memo

**Question.** Under R.C. 4511.194, is an intoxicated person "in physical control" of a vehicle in two factual variants: (A) passenger seat, engine off, keys in a closed glovebox; and (B) driver's seat, engine off, keys in a closed glovebox?

## I. The Statute

R.C. 4511.194(A)(2) defines "physical control" as **"being in the driver's position of the front seat of a vehicle . . . and having possession of the vehicle's . . . ignition key or other ignition device."** R.C. 4511.194(B)(1) makes it an offense for a person who satisfies that definition to be "under the influence of alcohol, a drug of abuse, or a combination of them." The offense is a first-degree misdemeanor. R.C. 4511.194(D). The statute became effective January 1, 2004 as part of Sub. S.B. 123 of the 124th General Assembly. It is one of two distinct alcohol-related offenses in Chapter 4511; the other is OVI under R.C. 4511.19, which differs from physical control only in that OVI requires "operation" — defined since 2004 as "to cause or have caused movement of a vehicle." R.C. 4511.01(HHH).

The two textual elements of physical control are conjunctive ("and"). Both must be satisfied. Neither term is defined further in R.C. 4511.194 itself, and the statute supplies no safe harbor.

"Possession" is not defined in R.C. 4511.194 or in the general definitions section of Chapter 4511. The criminal code's general provisions in R.C. 2901.21(F)(1) address possession only as a voluntary-act matter, providing that "[p]ossession is a voluntary act if the possessor knowingly procured or received the thing possessed, or was aware of the possessor's control of the thing possessed for a sufficient time to have ended possession." That is a *mens rea* clarification, not a substantive definition of the scope of possession. Ohio criminal practice generally recognizes both actual and constructive possession — i.e., that "[c]onstructive possession exists when an individual knowingly exercises dominion and control over an object, even though that object may not be within his immediate physical possession." *State v. Hankerson*, 70 Ohio St.2d 87, syllabus (1982). Whether the *Hankerson* concept of constructive possession imports cleanly into R.C. 4511.194 is unsettled at the appellate level, but as developed in Part II, Ohio courts have consistently treated keys "in close proximity" or "within reach" as sufficient for the operation/physical-control inquiry, which is functionally similar.

## II. The Ohio Case Law

### A. Foundational Supreme Court Authority (Pre-Statutory Offense)

Although R.C. 4511.194 is a 2004 statute, the term "physical control" has been a fixture of Ohio impaired-driving jurisprudence since 1976. The General Assembly drew the statute's elements directly from *Cincinnati v. Kelley*, 47 Ohio St.2d 94 (1976), which construed an identically worded Cincinnati municipal ordinance. The Ohio Supreme Court held that "actual physical control . . . requires that a person be in the driver's seat of a vehicle, behind the steering wheel, in possession of the ignition key, and in such condition that he is physically capable of starting the engine and causing the vehicle to move." *Id.* at 97–98 [HIGH]. The statutory definition in R.C. 4511.194(A)(2) is a near-verbatim codification of two of the four *Kelley* factors (driver's position; possession of ignition key).

In the years between *Kelley* and S.B. 123, the Supreme Court repeatedly broadened the parallel concept of "operation" under the former R.C. 4511.19, in a series the Court of Appeals later described as motivated by concern about "drunk radio listeners, or people who use their cars as a four-wheeled, heated hotel room." *State v. Schultz*, 2008-Ohio-4448, ¶ 19 (8th Dist.) [HIGH] (quoting Pfeifer, J., dissenting in *State v. Gill*). *State v. Cleary*, 22 Ohio St.3d 198 (1986), held that "a person in the driver's position in the front seat with the ignition key in his possession indicating either his actual or potential movement of the vehicle while under the influence of alcohol or any drug of abuse can be found in violation of R.C. 4511.19(A)(1)." *Id.* at 199 [HIGH]. *State v. Gill*, 70 Ohio St.3d 150 (1994), then made clear that "[a] clear purpose of R.C. 4511.19 is to discourage persons from putting themselves in the position in which they can potentially cause the movement of a motor vehicle while intoxicated . . . . Prohibition of potentially harmful conduct need not await occurrence of the act." *Id.* at 154 [HIGH]. *State v. McGlone*, 59 Ohio St.3d 122 (1991) [HIGH], held that key-in-ignition plus driver's seat sufficed for operation.

The S.B. 123 reforms in 2004 disentangled this case law: the legislature codified a narrow definition of "operate" (movement) in R.C. 4511.01(HHH), then created the new physical-control offense in R.C. 4511.194 to capture the no-movement scenarios that *Cleary*, *McGlone*, and *Gill* had previously swept into OVI. *State v. Wallace*, 166 Ohio App.3d 845, 2006-Ohio-2477, ¶¶ 8–14 (1st Dist.) [HIGH], traces this history.

### B. Post-2004 Construction of "Possession of the Ignition Key"

No Ohio Supreme Court decision has interpreted "possession" within R.C. 4511.194(A)(2). The construction of the term has been left to the courts of appeals, which have been remarkably consistent.

The most-cited post-2004 opinion is *Schultz* itself (cite count 22), where the Eighth District laid out the statutory framework. *Schultz* observed in footnote 6 that "the term 'operate' is still broad enough to encompass an impaired passenger who grabs the steering wheel of a moving vehicle. . . . **But an impaired passenger cannot be convicted of a physical control violation because he is not in the driver's seat. See R.C. 4511.194(A)(2)**." *Id.* fn. 6 [HIGH] (emphasis added).

The closest post-2004 case directly addressing keys-not-in-the-ignition is *Lakewood v. Zingale*, 2007-Ohio-2779 (8th Dist.) [HIGH], decided three years after the statute took effect. There, the defendant was found lying across the driver's seat into the passenger's seat with the keys in his right hand — not in the ignition, with the motor off, in a legally parked vehicle. The Eighth District held there was probable cause to arrest him for both OVI and physical control, and stated: "probable cause is not vitiated by the fact that appellant was legally parked, with the motor turned off, and the keys not in the ignition." *Id.* ¶ 23 [HIGH]. The court quoted approvingly from its earlier decision in *Broadview Heights v. Soukup*, Cuyahoga App. No. 64918 (Mar. 10, 1994) (unpublished but citable under Rep.Op.R. 3.4 if issued after May 1, 2002 — *Soukup* predates that date and is therefore persuasive only): "the ignition key of his vehicle need only be in close proximity to a defendant to constitute sufficient evidence defendant was operating his vehicle within the meaning of state DUI laws." *Zingale*, ¶ 20 [HIGH].

The pre-2004 court of appeals decisions on the same statutory phrase, decided under R.C. 4511.19 before the physical-control offense was severed out, point the same way. In *State v. Vanderkooi*, 82 Ohio App.3d 173 (3d Dist. 1992) [HIGH], the keys were retrieved by the officer from the back seat in plain view; the court held they were "within her reach from the driver's seat" and that "she had possession of the ignition key." *Id.* at 175. *State v. Barger*, 78 Ohio App.3d 451 (5th Dist. 1992) [MEDIUM — surfaced via cite-genealogy in *Latham* and *Vanderkooi*; snippet of holding not retrieved], reached the same conclusion where the defendant was in the driver's seat with the key in her possession but not in the ignition. *State v. Jenkins*, 75 Ohio App.3d 63, 70 (4th Dist. 1991) [HIGH], read the same way, then-uncertainly: "It does not appear that the Ohio Supreme Court has expressly held that R.C. 4511.19 is violated where the intoxicated driver is behind the wheel and has possession of the ignition key but it is not in the ignition." *Jenkins* surveyed the *Kelley*/*Cleary*/*McGlone* trilogy and concluded — in dissent dicta and concurring writings reviewed in subsequent panels — that out-of-ignition possession sufficed.

*State v. Kincaid*, 83 Ohio App.3d 341 (4th Dist. 1992) [HIGH], is the case at the outer edge: the defendant was asleep in the driver's seat with the key in the car ashtray. The lead opinion, applying the pre-2004 *McGlone* "key in the ignition" formulation literally, reversed the conviction; the concurrence — Judge Grey — would have held the ashtray sufficient. Note that *Kincaid* construed OVI under the old framework that required the key to be "in the ignition," a doctrinal element the 2004 statute discarded for physical control. The result in *Kincaid* would almost certainly come out the other way today under R.C. 4511.194.

In contrast, the most recent appellate snapshots — *State v. Miller*, 2024-Ohio-2009 (2d Dist.) [HIGH], and *State v. Adams*, 2024-Ohio-174 (2d Dist.) [HIGH] — apply R.C. 4511.194 routinely to drivers found unconscious in the driver's seat with keys in the vehicle (and, for *Miller*, "no evidence that the keys were in the ignition or the engine was running"). Neither decision questions that keys-within-the-cabin satisfies possession. *Miller* affirmed the OVI conviction on circumstantial evidence of operation, but its discussion of the OVI/physical-control distinction treats possession-within-the-vehicle as established by the keys being inside the cabin.

### C. Application to the Hypotheticals

**Variant A (passenger seat, engine off, keys in glovebox).** The defendant is **not** in physical control under R.C. 4511.194 because he fails the first textual element. The statute requires "being in the driver's position of the front seat of a vehicle." *Schultz* footnote 6 is directly on point: "an impaired passenger cannot be convicted of a physical control violation because he is not in the driver's seat." 2008-Ohio-4448, fn. 6 [HIGH]. The location of the keys is irrelevant to Variant A because the failure of the driver's-seat element is dispositive. This is a clean statutory acquittal on the physical-control charge. (The State could still pursue OVI under R.C. 4511.19 if it can prove operation — e.g., circumstantial evidence the defendant had earlier driven the vehicle to the location while impaired — but that is a different theory with a different element.)

**Variant B (driver's seat, engine off, keys in closed glovebox).** The defendant **is very likely** in physical control. The driver's-seat element is clearly satisfied. The possession element turns on whether keys inside a closed glovebox of one's own vehicle count as "possession of the vehicle's ignition key." No Ohio appellate decision squarely addresses keys-in-glovebox. The closest analogue is *Vanderkooi*, where keys "in plain view in the back seat" and "within reach from the driver's seat" sufficed. 82 Ohio App.3d at 175 [HIGH]. Keys in the driver's-side glovebox are within reach by any reasonable construction. The doctrinal purpose articulated in *Gill* — "to discourage persons from putting themselves in the position in which they can potentially cause the movement of a motor vehicle while intoxicated" — is fully implicated: the driver can open the glovebox and start the engine in seconds. 70 Ohio St.3d at 154 [HIGH].

A defense attorney could argue the closed glovebox is meaningfully different from in-hand or in-plain-view keys, particularly relying on the practitioner consensus that "if the key was in the trunk, held by another person, or otherwise inaccessible, the charge may not meet the statutory definition." That argument has surface plausibility but lacks Ohio appellate support, and the trunk analogy is weaker than it looks: a trunk is outside the cabin, requires the driver to exit the vehicle, and may require a separate key to access. A glovebox is inside the cabin, accessible from the driver's seat, and (under standard configurations) requires no additional key. Under *Cleary*'s "potential movement" rubric and *Zingale*'s "close proximity" language, the conviction should hold.

**Open factual variations within Variant B.** Two facts could change the analysis. (1) **Locked glovebox where the defendant lacks the key to open it.** A glovebox locked with a separate key, where the defendant does not have that key on his person, presents a genuinely undecided question — the keys are inside the vehicle, but functionally inaccessible to the defendant. There is no Ohio appellate case on this. (2) **The defendant cannot start the vehicle.** Where the vehicle is mechanically inoperable, the "physically capable of starting the engine" language from *Kelley* may apply by analogy, though it is not in the text of R.C. 4511.194. Cf. *Vanderkooi*, 82 Ohio App.3d at 175 [HIGH] (state must show vehicle "was capable of movement"). Neither limitation applies to the hypothetical as stated.

## III. Comparative Survey

State physical-control jurisprudence falls into four discernible camps along an axis from least to most expansive. Ohio sits in the middle.

### Camp 1: Bright-line statutory key-in-driver's-seat test (Ohio's model)

Ohio's R.C. 4511.194 is a defined-elements statute: driver's seat + possession of ignition key. A handful of other states use this same conjunctive bright-line architecture (e.g., Tennessee, Indiana). The advantage to defendants is that one missing element ends the analysis — as Variant A illustrates. The disadvantage is that within-element flexibility is doctrinal: "possession" expands to fill the gap through case law.

### Camp 2: Totality-of-circumstances multifactor test (the majority of states)

This is the dominant approach across the country, used in Arizona, Florida, Alabama, Pennsylvania, Maryland, California, Illinois, and many others. The leading articulation is the Arizona Supreme Court's decision in *State v. Zaragoza*, 221 Ariz. 49, 209 P.3d 629 (2009) [HIGH], which approved a non-exhaustive jury instruction listing factors including "whether the vehicle was running or the ignition was on; where the key was located; where and in what position the driver was found in the vehicle; whether the person was awake or asleep; if the vehicle's headlights were on; where the vehicle was stopped (in the road or legally parked); whether the driver had voluntarily pulled off the road; time of day and weather conditions; if the heater or air conditioner was on; whether the windows were up or down; and any explanation of the circumstances advanced by the defense." *Id.* at 53 [HIGH]. The touchstone is whether the defendant posed "a present or imminent risk of putting the vehicle in motion."

Florida is the other leading exemplar; its courts ask whether the defendant was "physically in or on the vehicle" and had "the capability to operate" it, evaluated under the totality of circumstances. Florida Std. Jury Instr. (Crim.) 28.1; *State v. Fitzgerald*, 63 So. 3d 75 (Fla. 2d DCA 2011) [HIGH]. *Fieselman v. State*, 537 So. 2d 603 (Fla. 1988) [MEDIUM — verified via `analyze_citations` against CourtListener (cluster 1130447); full opinion not retrieved], is widely treated as the foundational Florida decision in the totality-of-circumstances line.

**Application of the totality approach to Variant A:** passenger seat, engine off, keys in glovebox. A totality court could go either way, but on these facts (not in the driver's seat, vehicle off, keys not accessible without crossing into the driver's area) most totality jurisdictions would find no physical control. Arizona under *Zaragoza* would likely acquit; Florida would too.

**Application of the totality approach to Variant B:** driver's seat, engine off, keys in glovebox. A totality court would almost certainly find physical control. Driver's seat plus keys in immediate reach satisfies essentially every factor on the *Zaragoza* list.

### Camp 3: Statutory safe harbor (Washington model)

A small number of states create an explicit affirmative defense for the defendant who has chosen not to drive. The clearest example is RCW 46.61.504(2): "No person may be convicted under this section . . . if, prior to being pursued by a law enforcement officer, the person has moved the vehicle safely off the roadway." Washington still applies a broad totality test to the elements of the offense — physical control can be established even from the passenger seat with the engine running, as one recent Washington Court of Appeals decision held — but the safe harbor offers a defendant who has "pulled over to sleep it off" a way out. Wisconsin (statutorily) and Minnesota (judicially, in older cases) approached the problem somewhat similarly, though Minnesota's modern doctrine has narrowed.

**Application to the hypos:** Variant B in Washington would still satisfy the offense's elements (driver's seat is enough, with or without keys in the cabin). But if the defendant moved the car safely off the roadway *before* being pursued, the affirmative defense would defeat the conviction. Variant A would likely fail the offense's elements in any event because the defendant is in the passenger seat with the engine off and keys in the glovebox — although Washington case law has rejected a categorical passenger-seat rule.

### Camp 4: Expansive "imminent danger" test, no key required

A handful of older state decisions (and some current ones in jurisdictions without robust appellate guidance) extend physical control to defendants without keys at all, focusing entirely on the imminent risk of driving. The Maryland Court of Appeals' decision in *Atkinson v. State*, 331 Md. 199, 627 A.2d 1019 (1993) [MEDIUM — verified against CourtListener (cluster 2337244); full opinion not retrieved; relied on as represented in *Zaragoza*'s genealogy], is the most-cited articulation of the *Zaragoza*-style factor list and is treated by most commentators as the modern source of that approach rather than a Camp 4 outlier. Maryland's current articulation resembles the totality approach in *Zaragoza*. The true Camp 4 outliers today are older Mississippi and some pre-2000 South Dakota decisions, neither well-defined enough to anchor a flagship citation. As a doctrinal camp, this approach is no longer growing.

**Application to the hypos:** under a true Camp 4 approach, even Variant A could potentially generate physical control liability if the defendant was close to the vehicle and could be inferred to be planning to drive. This is the outer extreme.

## IV. What Remains Uncertain

Several propositions in this memo rest on uncertain ground:

1. **No Ohio appellate case squarely addresses keys-in-closed-glovebox under R.C. 4511.194.** The conclusion that Variant B states an offense is an inference from *Zingale* and *Vanderkooi*, both of which involve keys in plainer factual configurations (in hand; in plain view in back seat). The conclusion is well supported but not directly held.

2. **The "potential movement" rubric from *Cleary* and *Gill* was developed under the pre-2004 R.C. 4511.19 framework.** Whether the same purposive analysis governs post-2004 R.C. 4511.194 has not been squarely addressed by the Ohio Supreme Court. The Eighth District in *Zingale* and *Schultz* assumes continuity; this is reasonable but unconfirmed at the highest level.

3. **The relationship between *Hankerson*-style constructive possession and R.C. 4511.194's "possession of the ignition key" is unsettled.** Most physical-control opinions use the language of "close proximity" or "within reach" rather than the formal *Hankerson* test, and they do not import the *Hankerson* requirement of conscious awareness in any visible way. Whether a defendant who had truly forgotten the keys were in the glovebox could mount a *Hankerson* defense is undecided.

4. **The *Kincaid* outlier.** *Kincaid* is a 1992 Fourth District decision reversing an OVI conviction where the key was in the ashtray. Read literally — that "possession" requires the key to be in the ignition — *Kincaid* is in tension with everything decided since. *Kincaid* was decided under the pre-2004 statute and is best read as superseded by *Gill* and by the 2004 statutory reform, but no Ohio decision has formally overruled it. A defense attorney could try to use *Kincaid* in Variant B; the state would respond by pointing to *Zingale*, *Vanderkooi*, and *Barger*.

5. **Practitioner sources** uniformly say that keys "in the trunk" or "held by another person" defeat the offense. None of these claims is supported by any Ohio appellate decision I located; they appear to be defense-bar prudent practice. They should be treated as plausible defense theories, not as established Ohio law.

6. **Citability:** Ohio Rule for Reporting Opinions 3.4 makes all post-May-1-2002 Ohio appellate decisions citable. Pre-2002 unpublished decisions (e.g., *Soukup*) are not officially controlling and should be cited as persuasive only.
