Case Law Retrieval Benchmark v1

A 130-example federal-district leaderboard for closed-book legal case retrieval.

Sonnet 4.6 · Opus 4.7 · GPT-5  ·  Substance assessor: Opus 4.7  ·  130 unique propositions  ·  v1 results · May 2026

What was tested. Each model was given a legal proposition extracted from a real federal-district opinion's parenthetical and asked to name a federal case that supports it — closed-book, no tools, no web. Three axes graded each answer: does the cited case actually exist (CourtListener), does the named case match what CL resolved (similarity ≥ 0.65), and does the cited opinion substantively support the proposition (Opus 4.7 reading the opinion text, returning Green/Yellow/Red).

Headline. GPT-5 leads on Green rate (46% vs 36% Opus vs 31% Sonnet). At this sample size, only the Sonnet–GPT-5 difference has 95% CI excluding zero; the Opus–GPT-5 advantage is borderline and the Opus–Sonnet difference is well within noise.

Caveat. Sonnet's high UNKNOWN rate (52%, vs 27% Opus, 7% GPT-5) makes simple "hallucination rate" misleading: a model that refuses often answers fewer questions, but its few answers can still be wrong. The leaderboard surfaces the recall/precision tradeoff explicitly and reports F1 alongside the per-axis numbers.

Note on the dataset. A post-run audit found that the mining pipeline produced duplicate parentheticals (the same paren extracted multiple times from the same source opinion). After deduplication on (proposition, gold cite), the effective dataset is 130 unique propositions per model. All numbers below are deduped.

Three observations stand out. First, all three models do well on SCOTUS — the famous cases that get cited again and again in training data. Second, accuracy drops sharply at the circuit level and collapses at the district level: GPT-5 cited 5 district cases and not one was Green. The takeaway: GPT-5's headline lead is partly a confidence advantage on widely-cited cases, not necessarily a knowledge advantage on the long tail. A v1.1 dataset stratified by tier of cited case (forcing more district cases into the sample) is the natural next step.

Precision, recall, and F1

The Green-rate ranking flattens an underlying tradeoff. A model that refuses often answers fewer questions, but the few answers it gives can still be high-quality. To make that tradeoff explicit, treat the task as a retrieval problem:

  • True positive (TP): the model named a real case that substantively supports the proposition (Green).
  • False positive (FP): the model attempted an answer but the cited case is not real, doesn't match by name, or doesn't support (Yellow / Red / hallucinated / fake).
  • False negative (FN): the model returned UNKNOWN — declined to answer despite a supporting case existing (every proposition came from a real opinion's parenthetical, so by construction at least one supporter exists).

Under this framing:

F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall:  F1 = 2 · P · R / (P + R). It penalizes imbalance: a model that's strong on one axis but weak on the other scores low. Worked example for Sonnet (P=0.661, R=0.315): F1 = 2 × (0.661 × 0.315) / (0.661 + 0.315) = 0.427. Arithmetic mean of the same numbers would be 0.488 — substantially higher. Taken to the extreme, a model with 100% precision and 0% recall would score 50% under arithmetic mean and 0 under F1.

The reordering is informative. Sonnet has the highest precision (66%) — when it does name a case, it's most likely the right one — but its 52% UNKNOWN rate caps recall at 32%. GPT-5's precision is mid-pack (50%), but it answers nearly every prompt, so its recall stays high. Opus sits in the middle on both axes.

By F1, Sonnet (0.427) and Opus (0.418) are statistically indistinguishable; GPT-5 (0.478) leads. Which is the "best" model depends on use case: for a bench memo where every cite is checked, high precision matters more than coverage; for brainstorming candidate cases, recall dominates.

Methodology

Dataset construction

The dataset was assembled by sampling 200 explanatory parentheticals from federal-district opinions filed between January 1 and April 30, 2026 — after every tested model's training cutoff. The mining filter:

  • Queries CourtListener for opinions in five districts: D.D.C., N.D. Cal., S.D. Tex., N.D. Ill., and D. Mass. (D. Mass. substituted for S.D.N.Y. after S.D.N.Y. yielded zero opinions in the date range).
  • Extracts explanatory parentheticals ((holding/finding/explaining/...)) of 15–80 words.
  • Verifies the cited case resolves via CourtListener's citation-lookup API.
  • Stratifies the sample at 40 examples per district (200 total).

Each row pairs a real proposition (from the parenthetical) with the gold case the opinion cited. The benchmark question: can the model retrieve a federal case that supports the proposition? The original gold case is one valid answer, but not the only one — the substance assessor judges whether any federal case the model names actually supports.

Mining-stage duplication. The mining pipeline produced duplicate parentheticals (eyecite picking up the same citation in multiple forms — full reference and supra/id-style short citations — within a single source opinion). 70 of the 200 sampled rows were duplicates of other rows on the (proposition, gold cite) key. All numbers in this report use the deduplicated 130-proposition subset. v1.1 will dedup at the mining stage and re-mine to a clean N=200.

Tier distribution. Sample is stratified by citing court (40 propositions per district), but not by the tier of the cited case. Because district opinions overwhelmingly cite higher courts for binding precedent, the gold-case distribution is 60% circuit, 19% SCOTUS, 9% district, and 12% other (state courts, unpublished, etc.). This skew matters — see the per-tier results section above.

The skew compounds across two filtering stages, both biased against district cases:

The mining stage already underweights district cases (~18% of the raw pool, vs 45% circuit). Then CL verification drops a further 1 in 3 district cases — a 34% loss rate, roughly five times the loss for circuit (7%) or SCOTUS (0%). Random stratified-by-citing-court sampling to 130 thinned them again. Net result: district cases that the v1 sampling pipeline saw 56 of in the raw deduped data ended up as 18 in the final dataset.

This means v1's "district-cases-are-harder" finding (essentially zero Green when models attempt them) understates the problem in two ways. First, the n is too small to be statistically firm. Second, the district cases that did make it through are the easier-to-resolve ones — popular district opinions with clean reporter citations — so the few district cases models attempted were biased toward "should be findable." A v1.1 dataset that explicitly preserves the harder-to-resolve district cases (potentially via a multi-source existence oracle, since CL underperforms on them) would test the hypothesis directly.

The closed-book test

Each model received the same prompt template:

You are a legal research assistant. I will give you a legal proposition.
Name a US federal case that supports it.

Respond ONLY with the case name and citation, in this format:
<Case Name>, <Volume> <Reporter> <Page> (<Court> <Year>)

Do not include any explanation, parenthetical, or commentary. If you do
not know a supporting case, respond with "UNKNOWN".

Proposition: {proposition}

No web search, no tools, no retrieval — answers come from training memory only. Sonnet and Opus ran via the Claude CLI; GPT-5 ran via the OpenAI API directly.

Scoring (three axes)

  1. Real. Each model's cited reporter citation is sent to CourtListener's citation-lookup API. If CL finds a cluster, the citation is real.
  2. Name match. If real, the model's named case is compared against CL's resolved name with a multi-factor similarity score; threshold 0.65.
  3. Supports. For real cases, Opus 4.7 reads the cited opinion's text (first 20,000 characters) along with the proposition and the named case, and returns Green / Yellow / Red:
    • Green case directly and accurately supports the proposition
    • Yellow partially relevant; support is weaker than represented or the proposition slightly overstates the holding
    • Red case does not support; addresses a different topic; or no on-point passage exists in the excerpt

Confidence intervals

Pairwise differences in Green rate use a 5,000-sample bootstrap (independent resampling of each model's row pool, fixed seed 42). Independent resampling is conservative — paired bootstrap (resample example indices, evaluate both models on the same indices) would tighten CIs by removing within-example variance, but tends to favor "significant" findings, so the unpaired version is the safer choice.

Major caveats

CourtListener-coverage bias

Both the dataset's mining pass and the scoring step rely on CourtListener's citation-lookup API. Cases that exist but aren't well-resolved by CL — formatting edge cases, missing reporter parallels, older or state opinions — get marked NOT_FOUND even when they are real. The mining pass filters to CL-resolvable parentheticals, slightly under-representing propositions from harder-to-resolve gold cases (the unresolved pool is preserved in _raw_pool.json for future audit). Scoring uses CL's citation-lookup in "quick-only" mode (no opinion-search or PACER-RECAP fallback) — symmetric across the three models, so it doesn't bias the comparison, but a fuller search would catch some real cases marked NOT_FOUND today.

20,000-character opinion truncation — measured at 60K

The Opus assessor reads only the first 20K characters of each cited opinion (~5–7 pages); federal opinions are often 30–60+ pages, so support that lives later in the opinion gets missed and the case is marked Red. To quantify the bias, we re-ran the assessor on every v1 Red whose cached opinion exceeded 20K (59 of the 69 deduped Reds; the rest fit fully) at a 60K window — same prompt, same Opus model, same proposition. 22 of 59 flipped to Green or Yellow (37%): 12 → Green, 10 → Yellow.

If 60K had been the v1 default, headline Green rates would shift Sonnet 31.5% → 31.5% (all 3 Sonnet flips landed in Yellow), Opus 36.2% → 39.3%, GPT-5 46.2% → 52.4%. The correction widens rather than narrows the GPT-5 lead — the model with the most Reds had the most to gain. Big enough to call out; not big enough to reorder the leaderboard.

Two side findings. First, flip rates were nearly identical for SCOTUS (3/7, 43%) and circuit (16/39, 41%) Reds, so the SCOTUS-leans-easy pattern in Table 4 is a knowledge effect, not a syllabus-presence artifact. Second, District Reds did not flip at all (0/8) — adding text did not surface support that wasn't there, reinforcing the "models don't really know district cases" finding. Cost: ~$12 (Opus, 60K stdin prompts). Code: tests/benchmark_v1/truncation_experiment.py; data: benchmark_v1/truncation_experiment_60k.csv.

Closed-book only; no retrieval-augmented mode

Models answered from training memory alone. None had access to web search, RAG, or any tool. A model that knows it doesn't know a case has no way to look it up. Models with retrieval likely score very differently on these axes; v1.1 will add tool-augmented modes.

Temperature comparability

Sonnet and Opus run at the Claude CLI's provider default temperature; GPT-5 runs at OpenAI's provider default (1) because the API rejects temperature=0. This is a methodology footnote, not a comparability gate.

Sample size N=130 (effective, after dedup)

The Opus–Sonnet diff (+4.6pp) has CI [−6.9, +16.2] — straddles zero. The Opus–GPT-5 diff (−10.0pp) has CI [−21.5, +1.5] — also straddles zero, just barely. Only the Sonnet–GPT-5 diff has CI clearly excluding zero. Per-district numbers (n≈26 each after dedup) carry even wider uncertainty. v1.1 will fix the mining-stage duplication. Scaling to N=500 won't come cheap: v1.2's assessor-calibration study found that neither Sonnet 4.6 nor Haiku 4.5 reproduced Opus's verdicts well enough to replace it.

Reproducibility

Everything is open and committed. From a clean clone, with COURTLISTENER_API_TOKEN and OPENAI_API_KEY in .env and the Claude CLI installed:

venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/build_dataset.py     # ~30–60 min
venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/run_model.py --model sonnet
venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/run_model.py --model opus
venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/run_model.py --model gpt-5
venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/score.py             # ~3 hr
venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/scorecard.py            # original (inflated) numbers
venv/Scripts/python.exe tests/benchmark_v1/scorecard.py --dedupe   # corrected numbers (the ones cited above)

All five scripts are idempotent — interrupted runs resume. Bootstrap CIs use seed=42; rerunning scorecard.py reproduces the same intervals. Out-of-pocket cost: ~$10–15 for GPT-5 calls. Claude calls (model + assessor) consume Claude Code subscription quota (~700 calls' worth, ~$85 notional).

Limitations and future work

Each limitation in v1 has a planned fix. The list below ties the methodological gaps to the specific release that addresses them. The full roadmap is at docs/plans/benchmark-roadmap.md.

v1.1 — methodology hardening

  • Closed-book only. v1 measures memorized retrieval. Real legal practice uses RAG or web search. v1.1 adds tool-augmented and web-search modes so retrieval-equipped models can be measured against the same propositions.
  • Single existence oracle (CourtListener). Cases that exist but are awkwardly indexed in CL get marked NOT_FOUND. v1.1 adds a multi-source check (CL + Justia + Caselaw Access Project) so a model isn't penalized for citing a real case CL happens to render badly.
  • No good-law check. A model could correctly cite a case that's been overruled or vacated. v1.1 adds a "currency" axis using CL's citator data.
  • No jurisdictional-appropriateness check. Citing a 3rd Circuit case for a 5th Circuit proposition is a legal-research failure even if the case is real and on-point. v1.1 adds a court-hierarchy axis.
  • Forkable kit. v1's deliverable is a scorecard, not a toolkit. v1.1 ships SCHEMA.md, MINING_PLAYBOOK.md, QUALITY_GATES.md, etc. so other groups can run state-court or topic-specific variants.
  • Per-district CIs. The per-district table reports point estimates without intervals. v1.1 adds bootstrap CIs there too — useful for noticing where district sample size is too thin to draw conclusions.
  • Mining-stage deduplication. v1's mining pass produced ~10× duplication of each parenthetical inside its source opinion (eyecite picking up the same citation in full + short forms), leaving 35% of the 200-row dataset as duplicates of other rows. v1.1 will dedup at the mining stage on (citing_cluster_id, citation_text, parenthetical) before sending anything to CourtListener — saves API calls and prevents over-weighting in the final dataset.
  • Verified-citations cache. v1's gold cases are verified during the build phase, but scoring re-asks CourtListener for everything from scratch. v1.1 will introduce a citation → (cluster_id, case_name) cache, seeded from the gold-case verifications and grown across runs. Speeds re-runs, cuts CL dependency, and produces a diff-able artifact that lives in the repo.
  • Stratified sampling by tier of cited case. v1's dataset is 60% circuit, 19% SCOTUS, 9% district — driven by what district opinions actually cite, not by what the benchmark needs to test. v1.1 will mine a larger pool, then stratify the sample to include meaningful coverage of district cases (target ~33% per tier). This is what unlocks a serious "do models hallucinate district cases more often" claim instead of the n=4/n=5 hint we have now.
  • Per-case metadata extraction. v1 records the cited case's name, citation, and CL cluster ID, but not the court ID or filing year of the cited case. v1.1 adds a metadata-pull step (the cluster has both fields) and breaks down green / hallucination rates by jurisdiction and case age. Lets us answer questions like "do models hallucinate more on older cases?" and "is the circuit-split-on-the-9th-Circuit signal real?"

v1.2 — assessor calibration

  • Single substance assessor (Opus 4.7). The Green/Yellow/Red verdict comes from one model. Status (May 2026): the v1.2 calibration study tested Sonnet 4.6 and Haiku 4.5 against the deduped 257-cell subset under the same prompt at temperature=0. Both missed the bar by ~20pp on overall agreement and ~30pp on Red recall (Sonnet 68.9% / 52.2%; Haiku 65.4% / 55.1%). Sonnet's Red precision was high (87.8%) but its Red recall of 52.2% means roughly half the hallucinations Opus catches would be missed. Opus stays as primary assessor for v1.x; the 5–10× cost cut that would have unlocked N=500+ runs is not available.
  • 20K-character opinion truncation. The assessor reads the first 5–7 pages of each opinion. v1.2 will spot-check the long-opinion edge cases (federal opinions over 60 pages where the support might live deeper) and report how often raising the truncation changes the verdict.

v2 — scope expansion

  • Circuits and SCOTUS. v1 is districts only. Circuit-level parentheticals tend to be richer; SCOTUS would expose strong-recall vs. accuracy tradeoffs at higher difficulty.
  • State-law forks. The federal layer is the reference implementation. State-court variants (predicated on the v1.1 forkable kit) test how the methodology generalizes.
  • Larger N. N=130 (effective, after dedup) leaves both the Opus–Sonnet and Opus–GPT-5 diffs inside the noise floor. N=500 or 1000 would resolve them, but the cheap path via a smaller assessor is closed — v1.2's calibration study found neither Sonnet 4.6 nor Haiku 4.5 meets the agreement bar to replace Opus. Within Opus's budget envelope, N≈200 is the practical ceiling.

Outside the planned roadmap

  • Single eval per model. Each model answered each proposition once. Re-sampling per cell would tighten variance estimates but increases cost ~Nx. Not on the planned roadmap; would only matter if the calibration study (v1.2) suggests sampling variance is non-trivial.
