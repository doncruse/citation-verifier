46   III. Constitutional Framework and Clearly Established Law

47   The constitutional violations in this case implicate multiple clearly established rights that have been recognized

48   by the Supreme Court and circuit courts for decades:

49       First Amendment Retaliation: The Supreme Court in Nieves v. Bartlett established that the presence of

50   probable cause generally defeats a First Amendment retaliatory arrest claim, but recognized an important

51   exception where a plaintiff can demonstrate they were treated differently than similarly situated individuals

52   who did not engage in protected speech.5 The right to request badge numbers from police officers is clearly

53   established First Amendment activity,6 and retaliation for exercising this right violates clearly established

54   constitutional law.7

55       Fourth Amendment Excessive Force: The Supreme Court in Graham v. Connor established that all

56   excessive force claims must be analyzed under the Fourth Amendment’s objective reasonableness standard,

57   considering the totality of circumstances from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene.8 The

58   Graham factors include: (1) the need for the application of force; (2) the relationship between that need and

59   the amount of force used; (3) the extent of the injury inflicted; and (4) whether the force was applied in good

60   faith or maliciously and sadistically for the purpose of causing harm.9

61       Fourth Amendment Unreasonable Seizure: Under Terry v. Ohio, an investigative detention requires

62   reasonable suspicion that the individual has committed, is committing, or is about to commit a crime.10 A

63   traffic violation alone, without more, does not provide reasonable suspicion of criminal activity sufficient to

64   justify handcuffing and detention.11

65       Qualified Immunity Standard: Under Harlow v. Fitzgerald, government officials are entitled to qualified

66   immunity only if their conduct did not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a

67   reasonable person would have known.12 The contours of the right must be sufficiently clear that a reasonable

68   official would understand that what he is doing violates that right.13
