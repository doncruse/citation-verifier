Red: Unsupported or Misleading Citations (19)

Page 8: Sanctions imposed sua sponte without notice or hearing implicate due process and are reviewed de novo

Citing: Edwards v. Gen. Motors Corp., 153 F.3d 242, 246 (5th Cir. 1998)

Sanctions in Edwards were not sua sponte -- GM moved for fees and court held hearing. Court reviews 1927 sanctions for 'abuse of discretion,' not de novo. No discussion of de novo review for procedural deficiencies.

Page 11: Section 1927 must not be used to punish counsel for pursuing claims that ultimately fail

Citing: Morrison v. Walker, 939 F.3d 633, 637 (5th Cir. 2019)

Opinion does NOT state 1927 must not punish counsel for pursuing claims that ultimately fail. Opinion affirms sanctions against counsel precisely for pursuing a meritless claim.

Page 11: Informal communications and draft motions do not satisfy Rule 11(c)(2)'s mandatory service requirement

Citing: Deepwater Horizon v. Lake Eugenie Land & Dev., Inc., 857 F.3d 246, 250 (5th Cir. 2017)

This opinion (Freeh v. Lake Eugenie, 857 F.3d 246) is a Deepwater Horizon clawback fraud case. Contains NO discussion of Rule 11(c)(2), sanctions, or service requirements.

Page 12: Courts must not act as advocates when imposing sanctions

Citing: Deepwater Horizon v. Lake Eugenie Land & Dev., Inc., 857 F.3d 246, 250 (5th Cir. 2017)

Opinion (857 F.3d 246) does not address sanctions at all. Case concerns clawback of BP settlement funds.

Page 13: The procedural safeguards required under section 1927 and the court's inherent powers were violated

Citing: Edwards v. Gen. Motors Corp., 153 F.3d 242, 246 (5th Cir. 1998)

Court affirmed 1927 sanctions. Reversed only Rule 11 sanctions based on inapplicability to pre-removal state filings, not procedural deficiencies.

Page 13: Government-issued fraud documentation triggers evidentiary presumption

Citing: United States v. Miller, 953 F.3d 1095, 1101 (9th Cir. 2020)

United States v. Miller is a wire fraud/embezzlement case. No discussion of 'government-issued fraud documentation' or 'evidentiary presumption.' Word 'presumption' does not appear.

Page 13: Frivolousness must be assessed at the time of filing not in hindsight

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826, 834-36 (2011)

Fox does NOT address timing of frivolousness assessment. Opinion concerns fee allocation under 1988 but-for test. The brief's alleged quote 'The question is whether the case was frivolous when filed, not whether it later proved to be so' does NOT appear anywhere in the opinion.

Page 13: Rule 11 sanctions are to be imposed only for arguments that are frivolous when made not for those that later prove unsuccessful

Citing: Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp., 496 U.S. 384, 393 (1990)

Brief quotes at 393: 'Rule 11 sanctions are to be imposed only for arguments that are frivolous when made, not for those that later prove unsuccessful.' This exact quote does NOT appear. Page 393 discusses Rule 11 history.

Page 14: Retroactive justification of sanctions is barred; contemporaneous scrutiny is required

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826 (2011)

Fox does NOT address retroactive justification or contemporaneous scrutiny. Opinion solely about allocating fees under 1988 when plaintiff asserts both frivolous and non-frivolous claims.

Page 14: Frivolousness must be judged at filing not reconstructed after the fact

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826 (2011)

Fox does NOT address timing of frivolousness. Court assumes claims already found frivolous and addresses only allocation. Proposition unsupported.

Page 15: Sanctions imposed without formal notice briefing or evidentiary hearing constitute structural error

Citing: Puckett v. United States, 556 U.S. 129, 135 (2009)

Puckett is about forfeited plea-agreement breach under Rule 52(b) plain-error review. Does not discuss sanctions at all.

Page 16: Due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before sanctions are imposed

Citing: Deepwater Horizon v. Lake Eugenie Land & Dev., Inc., 857 F.3d 246, 250 (5th Cir. 2017)

The quoted language 'Due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before sanctions are imposed' does NOT appear. Case is about Deepwater Horizon settlement clawback, not sanctions.

Page 17: Sanctions imposed without formal notice briefing or evidentiary hearing are reviewed de novo because they implicate core constitutional safeguards

Citing: Edwards v. Gen. Motors Corp., 153 F.3d 242, 246 (5th Cir. 1998)

Sanctions imposed after hearing on GM's motion. Court applied abuse of discretion: 'We review the ruling only for abuse of discretion.' No de novo review for procedural deficiencies discussed.

Page 18: Courts must segregate fees incurred defending frivolous claims from those incurred defending non-frivolous ones

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion does NOT discuss segregating fees between frivolous and non-frivolous claims. Only one claim in Vaughan, found not frivolous. Remand instruction concerns fees from specific deposition misconduct, not segregation across claims of varying merit.

Page 19: Experian's silence distorted the compensable frame and undermined the adversarial process

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826, 836 (2011)

Fox contains nothing about party silence, distortion of compensable frames, or adversarial process. Opinion is about fee allocation methodology.

Page 19: Experian's silence distorted the compensable frame and undermined the adversarial process

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion contains no discussion of 'silence distorting the compensable frame' or 'undermining the adversarial process.' Case is about VRA lawsuit frivolity and deposition misconduct. Nothing supports this proposition.

Page 19: The district court must segregate fees incurred defending frivolous claims from those incurred defending non-frivolous ones

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Same as Row 26. Opinion does not hold courts 'must segregate fees incurred defending frivolous claims from those incurred defending non-frivolous ones.' Single non-frivolous claim; remand concerns deposition misconduct fees only.

Page 27: Sanctions order violates Rule 11(c)(2) and rests on post hoc justification barred by controlling precedent

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826 (2011)

Fox does not address Rule 11(c)(2) at all. Opinion concerns 1988 fee-shifting, not Rule 11 sanctions. Rule 11 never mentioned.

Page 28: Fox and Cooter bar retroactive justification of sanctions

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826 (2011)

Fox does not bar retroactive justification. Opinion addresses only fee allocation under 1988 but-for test.

Yellow: Partially Supported or Overstated (28)

Page 5: The district court's fee award violates Fifth Circuit mandate requiring segregation of fees

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Vaughan requires on remand identifying fees 'incurred because' of sanctionable conduct, with findings that 'identify sanctionable conduct and distinguish it from the reasons for deciding the case on the merits' and 'link the sanctionable conduct to the size of the sanctions' (citing Procter & Gamble). But does not use 'segregation of fees' and context is narrow (deposition misconduct under 1927), not a broad mandate.

Page 6: Sanctions order violates Rule 11(c)(2) and rests on post hoc justification barred by controlling precedent

Citing: Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp., 496 U.S. 384 (1990)

Cooter & Gell addresses Rule 11 broadly but does not discuss Rule 11(c)(2) (created by 1993 amendments, 3 years after this 1990 decision) or 'post hoc justification.' Temporal-anchoring at *404 provides indirect support.

Page 6: Sanctions order awards fees without causation segregation required by Fifth Circuit precedent

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199 (5th Cir. 2023)

Vaughan requires 1927 sanctions tied to specific conduct: court 'may award only those fees and costs associated with the persistent prosecution of a meritless claim' and must identify fees 'incurred because' of misconduct. But context is deposition misconduct, not general 'causation segregation.' Proposition overstates.

Page 7: Sanctions must not deter legitimate advocacy

Citing: Coghlan v. Starkey, 852 F.2d 806, 809 (5th Cir. 1988)

Coghlan notes 'this court has no desire to deter any litigant from advancing any claim or defense arguably supported by existing law.' But context is Rule 38, and opinion's thrust is pro-sanctions.

Page 9: Section 1927 does not reach the filing of a single non-frivolous pleading supported by evidence

Citing: Peterson v. BMI Refractories, 124 F.3d 1386, 1396 (11th Cir. 1997)

Peterson reverses 1927 sanctions for lack of causal nexus. But does not hold 1927 'does not reach filing of single non-frivolous pleading.' Sanctioned conduct was pattern of communication failures.

Page 9: Sanctions are an extraordinary remedy reserved for conduct that is both objectively unreasonable and undertaken in bad faith

Citing: Morrison v. Walker, 939 F.3d 633, 637 (5th Cir. 2019)

Standard is 'reckless disregard of duty,' NOT 'bad faith.' Brief's 'bad faith' characterization overstates Morrison's holding -- reckless disregard is lower threshold.

Page 14: Retroactive justification of sanctions is barred; contemporaneous scrutiny is required

Citing: Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp., 496 U.S. 384 (1990)

Does not use 'retroactive justification' or 'contemporaneous scrutiny.' Temporal-anchoring at *404 implies inquiry at filing time, but proposition is extrapolation, not direct holding.

Page 14: Frivolousness must be judged at filing not reconstructed after the fact

Citing: Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp., 496 U.S. 384 (1990)

Supports temporal anchoring at *404 but language appears in context of appellate review standard, not standalone holding.

Page 16: Structural errors affect the framework within which the trial proceeds and are not subject to harmless error review

Citing: Puckett v. United States, 556 U.S. 129, 135 (2009)

Phrase 'affect the framework within which the trial proceeds' does appear (quoting Fulminante). But at p.141 not p.135, and Court holds plea breach is NOT structural error -- brief inverts the holding.

Page 19: The court awarded a flat percentage based on billing records violating the compensatory purpose of section 1927

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion requires fees causally linked to sanctionable conduct: 'link the sanctionable conduct to the size of the sanctions.' Consistent with compensatory purpose, but does not discuss 'flat percentage based on billing records.'

Page 20: The fee award bundles unrelated litigation tasks and lacks line-item causation findings violating Fifth Circuit mandate

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion requires findings that '(1) identify sanctionable conduct, (2) link the sanctionable conduct to the size of the sanctions, (3) differentiate between sanctions under different statutes' (citing Procter & Gamble). Supports causation findings, but never uses 'line-item causation findings.'

Page 20: The fee award bundles unrelated litigation tasks and lacks line-item causation findings violating Supreme Court directive

Citing: Fox v. Vice, 563 U.S. 826, 836 (2011)

Fox at 836 establishes but-for test requiring causal link. But does not use 'line-item causation findings' and Court says trial courts may 'use estimates in calculating and allocating an attorney's time' (at 838).

Page 21: The Fifth Circuit requires line-item segregation of fees caused by sanctionable conduct

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion requires 1927 sanctions linked to specific conduct and identification of fees incurred 'because of' misconduct. Supports causation requirement, but never uses 'line-item segregation.'

Page 21: Bulk allocation of 60% of total fees without isolating causation violates fee segregation requirements

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion's requirement that sanctions be linked to specific conduct is inconsistent with bulk allocation. Underlying principle supported, but opinion does not address '60% bulk allocation.'

Page 21: The absence of line-item segregation renders the award non-compensatory

Citing: Vaughan v. Lewisville Indep. Sch. Dist., 62 F.4th 199, 207 (5th Cir. 2023)

Opinion requires causal connection to sanctionable conduct. Consistent with compensatory nature, but does not use 'non-compensatory' or state absence of line-item segregation per se renders award invalid.

Page 22: Sanctions must not be used as mere fee-shifting devices

Opinion states Rule 11 is not fee-shifting (*409). But pinpoint at 397 is wrong -- page 397 discusses Rule 41(a)(1). Fee-shifting language at *409.

Page 23: Sanctions under Rule 11 are collateral to the merits and may be imposed after dismissal
