p. 3 — Tompkins v. Cyr, 202 F.3d 770, 787 (5th Cir. 2000) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
"Courts consistently and uniformly hold that evidence of prior settlement or verdict amounts is irrelevant to the issues of liability and damages in a subsequent, unrelated accident. See Tompkins v. Cyr, 202 F.3d 770, 787 (5th Cir. 2000) (evidence must be relevant to a 'consequential fact' in the case at bar)."
What the opinion actually says:
Tompkins v. Cyr is a case about anti-abortion protesters who harassed a doctor. It involves RICO claims, defamation, and emotional distress damages. The phrase "consequential fact" does not appear anywhere in the opinion. The relevance discussions in the case concern whether anonymous threats and a murder in Florida were relevant to the plaintiffs' emotional distress claims. There is no discussion of prior settlement amounts, verdict amounts, or unrelated accident evidence.
Assessment: This citation does not support the proposition it is cited for. The case has nothing to do with excluding prior settlement or verdict evidence in subsequent litigation. The parenthetical characterization ("evidence must be relevant to a 'consequential fact' in the case at bar") does not appear in the opinion. This could reflect a citation error (wrong case or wrong reporter page) or a fabricated attribution.
p. 3 — Collins v. Wayne Corp., 621 F.2d 777, 784 (5th Cir. 1980) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
"Collins v. Wayne Corp., 621 F.2d 777, 784 (5th Cir. 1980) (excluding evidence that had no bearing on the specific issues before the jury)."
What the opinion actually says:
Collins v. Wayne Corp. is a products liability action arising from a bus-truck collision that killed 19 people. Page 784 discusses cross-examination of an expert witness about fees earned in prior cases and whether the plaintiffs preserved error by failing to object. The phrase "excluding evidence that had no bearing on the specific issues before the jury" does not appear. There is no discussion of excluding prior settlement or verdict amounts from unrelated cases.
Assessment: This citation does not support the proposition it is cited for. The case involves entirely different evidentiary issues (expert witness cross-examination in a products liability trial). It does not establish any principle about excluding prior settlement or verdict evidence.
p. 11 — United States v. Abel, 469 U.S. 45, 52 (1984) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
"The Supreme Court has addressed the proper scope of bias evidence, emphasizing that such evidence must demonstrate actual bias affecting the witness's testimony. United States v. Abel, 469 U.S. 45, 52 (1984)."
What the opinion actually says:
Abel holds that bias evidence is broadly admissible. The Court wrote: "Proof of bias is almost always relevant because the jury, as finder of fact and weigher of credibility, has historically been entitled to assess all evidence which might bear on the accuracy and truth of a witness' testimony." The case upheld admission of evidence that a defense witness and the defendant were members of the same prison gang, as this tended to show the witness's bias. The opinion stands for the breadth of admissible bias evidence, not for restricting it.
Assessment: The brief mischaracterizes Abel as establishing a restrictive standard for bias evidence — that "such evidence must demonstrate actual bias affecting the witness's testimony." In fact, Abel holds the opposite: evidence that makes bias more probable is sufficient, and trial courts have broad discretion to admit such evidence. The brief cites this case to argue that attorney-referral evidence should be excluded unless it shows "actual bias," but Abel would more naturally support the admission of such evidence. An opposing counsel familiar with Abel could use this mischaracterization to undermine the brief's argument.

Assessment: The substance is accurate — the opinion clearly states that Rule 408 aims to encourage settlements. However, the brief places in quotation marks the phrase "the policy behind Rule 408 is to encourage settlements," when the opinion actually says "The purpose of Rule 408 is to encourage settlements." p. 9 — Michelson v. United States, 335 U.S. 469, 475–76 (1948) — Overstated — case partially supports
What the brief claims:
"The Supreme Court has long recognized the prejudicial impact of arrest evidence where no conviction exists. Michelson v. United States, 335 U.S. 469, 475–76 (1948)."
What the opinion actually says:
Pages 475–76 discuss the general rules about character evidence — specifically that the prosecution cannot introduce evidence of a defendant's evil character to establish guilt, because such evidence "weigh[s] too much with the jury" and tends to "prejudge one with a bad general record." The specific discussion of arrest evidence (as opposed to conviction) appears later in the opinion, where Justice Jackson writes: "Arrest without more does not, in law any more than in reason, impeach the integrity or impair the credibility of a witness. It happens to the innocent as well as the guilty."
Assessment: Michelson does address the prejudicial impact of arrest evidence, but not at the cited pages 475–76. Those pages discuss character evidence rules generally. The arrest-specific language appears later in the opinion. The proposition is substantively supported by the case as a whole, but the pinpoint citation is inaccurate.

What the opinion actually says:
"It is clear that a defendant takes his victim as he finds him and is responsible for all natural and probable consequences of his tortious conduct."
Assessment: The only difference is "The tortfeasor" in the brief versus "a defendant" in the opinion. The meaning is identical — "tortfeasor" and "defendant" are synonymous in this context. This is a very minor rewording, but the quotation marks in the brief suggest a verbatim quote when it is not quite verbatim. The opinion also uses "tortfeasor" elsewhere (Section III), so the word is not foreign to the opinion — just not used in this specific sentence.

Methodology & Limitations

  • This report checks whether cited cases say what the brief claims they say. It does not assess the overall legal merit of the brief's arguments or whether the cited cases are the best authorities available.
