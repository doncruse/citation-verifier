7        1.   Relevance                                                   
8        The privileged information is relevant.  On June 11, 2024, Evolve and its parent company 
9   entered into a consent decree with the FRB and ASBD.  The consent decree stated that the 
10   regulators identified deficiencies in Evolve’s risk management of OBD and other deficiencies in 
11   reports dated August 11, 2023, August 30, 2023, and January 10, 2024.  The consent decree 
12   required Evolve to submit a written plan that would broadly address compliance and risk in 
13   Evolve’s OBD, including, among many other things, written policies and procedures to manage 
14   compliance and fraud risks with each fintech partner, product, program, service, business line or 
15   customer.

23   an email to Moelis that problems with Synapse were merely the result of ‘some contractual issues 
24   with Synapse’ that were ‘being handled’ in a manner that would ‘protect end users’”; “Written 
25   representations by Lenoir, on or about October 3, 2023, in an email to Moelis, that problems with 
26   Synapse were merely the result of ‘some contractual issues with Synapse,’ and that Yotta could 
27   ‘transition to another bank’ should it or its users desire to do so”; “Evolve’s written representation, 
1   user accounts.’”).  Further, Evolve is invoking privilege over documents going back to 2018, ECF 
2   No. 134-1 (first two entries).  Accordingly, it is very unlikely that Evolve first learned of the 
3   widespread deficiencies in its OBD on the day the regulators issued their reports. 
4        Yotta thinks that the privileged documents will show that Evolve knew that its many 
5   representations to Yotta over the years concerning the soundness of its banking operations were 
6   false.  The Court agrees with Yotta.  Accordingly, the documents are relevant, and this factor 
7   favors disclosure.                                                    
8        2.   Availability of Other Information                           
9        Yotta has been able to take full discovery in this case.  This factor counts against Yotta.  
10   Yotta does not articulate any area of relevant discovery that it was not able to explore through the 
11   regular discovery process.  Yotta argues that “[t]he Consent Decree indicates that the Bank 
12   Examinations found serious problems with Evolve’s OBD.”  But “it is hard to reconcile plaintiff’s 
13   assertions that the Bank’s misconduct was ‘widespread’ and evidenced only by communications 
14   with the [regulator].”  In re B of I Holding, 2022 WL 507663, *6 (emphasis added).  Yotta argues 
15   that “this information is not available elsewhere as Evolve appears to have withheld all 
16   documents, including internal discussions, related to the bank examinations.”  Of course the 
17   withheld documents are not themselves available elsewhere; they were withheld.  The relevant 
18   inquiry is whether factual information that is relevant to the case is contained only in the 
19   privileged documents.  Yotta has not made or attempted to make that showing. 
20        3.   Seriousness of the Litigation                               
21        This factor does not favor disclosure.  This lawsuit is serious to Yotta, of course, because it 
22   concerns the collapse of its business.  But Yotta is not a bank, and this lawsuit is not about a bank 
23   collapse.  It is a dispute between two private parties where money damages are the remedy.  See In 
24   re Wilmington Trust Secs. Litig., 2016 WL 9753979, *10 (D.Del. Aug. 16, 2016) (“ In Shirk, the 
25   court determined that an ERISA action between private litigants where money damages were the 
26   remedy did not rise to the level of seriousness which would require production of confidential 
27   bank examination materials.  The failure of a bank, however, has more widespread implications 
1   preclude a meaningful comparison.”).                                  
2        4.   Role of the Government                                      
3        The government is not a party to this case and does not have a direct interest in its 
4   outcome.  “The Court finds that this factor weighs against disclosure.”  In re B of I Holding, 2022 
5   WL 507663, *7.                                                        
6        5.   Chilling Effect                                             
7        The broad sweep of Yotta’s motion seems to maximize the chilling effect it would have.  
8   Yotta says it is not moving to compel every privileged document, only the ones that include 
9   discussion of Evolve’s insufficient controls, policies, or practices with respect to its OBD.  But 
10   given the subject matter of the consent decree, the Court thinks that is not likely much of a 
11   limitation at all.  Yotta is basically moving to compel every communication with the regulators 
12   over a span of several years.  This broad disclosure is more likely to have a chilling effect than a 
13   narrow disclosure of just a few documents.                            
14        6.   Conclusion                                                  
15        Overall, the weight of the factors counsels against overruling the qualified privileges.  
16   Accordingly, Yotta’s motion to compel is DENIED.

7        The Court is not going to order this.  As you will recall, document production in this case

10   deadline should be was its burden in redacting PII.  Evolve said:  “given the way the Synapse-
11   related accounts were structured, there is a significant PII in the potentially responsive documents 
12   for non-Yotta customers that require redaction. . . . Evolve is a federally regulated and Arkansas-
13   chartered bank and must comply with federal and state privacy laws.”  ECF No. 95 at 3 (emphasis 
14   omitted).  The Court’s order observed that “ Evolve says there is significant PII in the potentially 
15   responsive documents that needs to be redacted under federal and state privacy laws,” ECF No. 97 
16   at 1.                                                                 
17        So, Yotta knew Evolve would be redacting PII.  Yotta then waited until 39 days after the 
18   substantial completion deadline, and on the last day of fact discovery filed this motion to force 
19   Evolve to produce all the PII.  While this motion is technically compliant with the deadline to file 
20   a discovery motion, see Civ. L.R. 37-3, redoing a significant part of Evolve’s document 
21   production like this is not proportional to the needs of the case, especially when Evolve made clear 
22   that redacting the PII in the first instance required a lot of work.  Discovery motions brought at the 
23   end of fact discovery should be about cleaning up loose ends, not redoing things from the 
24   beginning.  This is especially true when Yotta knew that Evolve would be redacting PII in its 
25   document production and waited until long after that production was made to file this motion.  
26   Yotta’s motion to compel is DENIED.

12        4.   Linked Documents                                            
13        Finally, Yotta moves to compel the documents that are hyperlinked in the produced 
14   documents listed in ECF No. 139-2.  The Court agrees with Evolve that a hyperlink relationship 
15   (meaning the document, such as an email, contains a link to another document) does not by itself 
16   mean the linked document should be treated as an attachment and produced.  See In re Meta Pixel 
17   Healthcare Litig., 2023 WL 4361131, *1 (N.D. Cal. June 2, 2023) (“[T]he ESI protocol should 
18   make clear that hyperlinked documents are not treated as conventional attachments for purposes of 
19   preserving a ‘family’ relationship in production.”); Nichols v. Noom Inc., 2021 WL 948646, *4 
20   (S.D.N.Y. March 11, 2021) (“[T]he Court does not agree that a hyperlinked document is an 
21   attachment.”).

23   the nature of the produced document.  See In re Meta Pixel Healthcare Litig., 2023 WL 4361131,

25   collect) hyperlinked documents and associate them with the underlying ESI in which the links 
26   appear.  In such circumstances, the parties should consider reasonable requests for production of 
27   hyperlinked documents on a case-by-case basis.  Such requests should not be made as a matter of
