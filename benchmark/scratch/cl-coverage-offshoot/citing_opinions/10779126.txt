2   II.  DISCUSSION                                                       
3        The Court adopts Judge Cisneros’ report and recommendations in full.  The Court 
4   agrees that the Court is unable to hear the claims against the Illinois Defendants and that 
5   Hendrix fails to state a claim against the California Defendants.  Consequently, the Court 
6   denies Hendrix’s administrative motion as moot.  The Court also denies Hendrix’s motion 
7   for a recusal as he has not demonstrated an extrajudicial source of bias.  In light of 
8   Hendrix’s concerns with his public alias, the Court has sealed the current filings that 
9   contain his alias.  The Court explains its reasoning on the report and recommendations and 
10   the motion for recusal in further detail below.                       
11        A.   Report and Recommendations                                  
12        Judge Cisneros identifies three grounds for dismissal as to the Illinois Defendants: 
13   preclusion, the Rooker-Feldman doctrine, and lack of personal jurisdiction.  R&R at 5–7.  
14   The Court agrees with each one.                                       
15        Preclusion bars Hendrix’s claims against the judges among the Illinois Defendants.  
16   Claim preclusion “provides that a final judgment on the merits bars further claims by 
17   parties or their privies based on the same cause of action, and is central to the purpose for 
18   which civil courts have been established, the conclusive resolution of disputes within their 
19   jurisdiction.”  Headwaters Inc. v. U.S. Forest Serv., 399 F.3d 1047, 1051–52 (9th Cir. 
20   2005) (cleaned up).  And “[n]ewly articulated claims based on the same nucleus of facts 
21   may still be subject to a res judicata finding if the claims could have been brought in the 
22   earlier action.”  Tahoe-Sierra Pres. Council, Inc. v. Tahoe Reg’l Plan. Agency, 322 F.3d 
23   1064, 1078 (9th Cir. 2003).  The related doctrine of issue preclusion, which is also known 
24   as collateral estoppel and also falls under the umbrella of res judicata, provides that 
25   “[w]hen an issue of fact or law is actually litigated and determined by a valid and final 
26   judgment, and the determination is essential to the judgment, the determination is 
27   conclusive in a subsequent action between the parties, whether on the same or a different 
1   omitted).                                                             
2        As Judge Cisneros noted, Hendrix’s claims and allegations here functionally mirror 
3   those he made against the Illinois judges in their home state, which were dismissed for a 
4   lack of subject matter jurisdiction based, in part, on judicial immunity.  R&R at 6 (noting 
5   that the Seventh Circuit affirmed dismissal); Hohu v. Hatch, 940 F. Supp. 2d 1161 (N.D. 
6   Cal. 2013) (dismissal based on lack of subject matter jurisdiction has a preclusive effect in 
7   the Ninth Circuit).1  Accordingly, Hendrix is precluded from relitigating judicial immunity 
8   against the Illinois judges.  See Obj. at 4.                          
9        Moreover, outside of preclusion, the Rooker-Feldman doctrine would bar Hendrix’s 
10   claims against all the Illinois Defendants.2  At bottom, Hendrix’s complaint seeks to 
11   relitigate state proceedings he contends were conducted based on fraud.  See Compl. at 2.  
12   Hendrix attempts avoid this doctrinal bar by arguing that the state order itself was void for 
13   lack of jurisdiction.  Obj. at 3.  But as Judge Cisneros noted, the no-contact order was 
14   before Hendrix removed the case to federal court.  R&R at 8.  And to the extent Hendrix 
15   argues the subsequent extension of the no-contact order was without jurisdiction, the 
16   complaint does not specify whether the extension was during the five days before remand 
17   back to state court.  Id.  Regardless, such an issue, if true, should have been brought up in 
18   Illinois, not through relitigation in this Court.                     
19        Additionally, Judge Cisneros is correct that the complaint offers “no basis for this 
20   Court to exercise personal jurisdiction over any of the Defendants in Illinois.”  R&R at 7 
21   (emphasis in original); see Pebble Beach Co. v. Caddy, 453 F.3d 1151, 1154 (9th Cir. 
22   2006) (a court can only hear a case if it has personal jurisdiction over the defendant).

3        The claims against the California Defendants fare no better.  The complaint only 
4   includes a single sentence: “When Plaintiff later sought CAAP benefits, Defendants 
5   City/County of San Francisco, Williams, Jordan, and Ly failed to provide required 
6   reasonable disability accommodations, resulting in wrongful termination of benefits in

15        Hendrix’s other objections are also unavailing.  The denial of the TRO is unrelated 
16   to the report and recommendations.  Further, Hendrix’s objections to Dkt. 12 (the return of 
17   mail sent to Hendrix) and to Judge Cisneros listing Hendrix’s aliases do not address the 
18   substance of the report and recommendations and the deficiencies in the pleadings.3  And 
19   Judge Cisneros’ denial of service at this point was justified given the recommendation to 
20   dismiss.  If Hendrix is able to state a claim with an amended complaint, he may be entitled 
21   to an extended time for service.  Fed. R. Civ. P. 4(m) (providing extension of service 
22   period on a showing of good cause).                                   
23        The Court adopts the report and recommendations and dismisses the case. 
24   III.  MOTION FOR RECUSAL

26   denies the motion.
