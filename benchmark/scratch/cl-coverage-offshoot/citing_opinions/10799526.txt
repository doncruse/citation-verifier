11        Plaintiff, a pretrial detainee facing state criminal charges in San Mateo County Superior 
12   Court, has filed a pro se complaint under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 alleging that his court-appointed 
13   “private defender” John Elsworth denied him his constitutional right to call the main defense 
14   witness in the criminal case against him.  Plaintiff seeks injunctive relief/federal court intervention 
15   and damages from Elsworth and the Private Defender Program (PDP).     
16                               DISCUSSION                                
17   A.   Standard of Review                                               
18        Federal courts must engage in a preliminary screening of cases in which prisoners seek 
19   redress from a governmental entity or officer or employee of a governmental entity.  28 U.S.C. § 
20   1915A(a).  The court must identify cognizable claims or dismiss the complaint, or any portion of 
21   the complaint, if the complaint “is frivolous, malicious, or fails to state a claim upon which relief 
22   may be granted,” or “seeks monetary relief from a defendant who is immune from such relief.”  Id. 
23   § 1915A(b).  Pro se pleadings must be liberally construed, however.  Balistreri v. Pacifica Police 
24   Dep’t, 901 F.2d 696, 699 (9th Cir. 1990).                             
25        To state a claim under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, a plaintiff must allege two elements:  (1) that a 
26   right secured by the Constitution or laws of the United States was violated, and (2) that the alleged 
27   violation was committed by a person acting under the color of state law.  West v. Atkins, 487 U.S. 
1   B.   Legal Claims                                                     
2        It is well established that federal courts should not enjoin or otherwise interfere with 
3   pending state criminal prosecutions absent a showing of the state’s bad faith or harassment, or a 
4   showing that the statute challenged is flagrantly and patently violative of express constitutional 
5   prohibitions.  See Younger v. Harris, 401 U.S. 37, 43-54 (1971) (cost, anxiety and inconvenience 
6   of criminal defense not kind of special circumstances or irreparable harm that would justify federal 
7   court intervention; statute must be unconstitutional in every clause, sentence and paragraph, and in 
8   whatever manner it is applied).  The rationale of Younger applies throughout state appellate 
9   proceedings, requiring that state appellate review of a state court judgment be exhausted before 
10   federal court intervention is permitted.  See Huffman v. Pursue, Ltd., 420 U.S. 592, 607-11 
11   (1975).                                                               
12        Here, the rationale of Younger requires that this court abstain from entertaining plaintiff’s 
13   request for injunctive relief/federal court intervention in his pending state criminal proceedings 
14   unless plaintiff  shows that special circumstances warrant federal court intervention.  Cf. Carden v. 
15   Montana, 626 F.2d 82, 83-84 (9th Cir. 1980) (pre-sentence habeas action).  Only in cases of 
16   proven harassment or prosecutions undertaken by state officials in bad faith without hope of 
17   obtaining a valid conviction and perhaps in other special circumstances where irreparable injury 
18   can be shown is federal intervention in ongoing state criminal proceedings appropriate.  Cf. id. at 
19   84 (citing Perez v. Ledesma, 401 U.S. 82, 85 (1971)).  Plaintiff makes no such showing of special 
20   circumstances.  His broad allegations of denial of a key defense witness are not enough to warrant 
21   federal court intervention.  The claim may be raised in the ongoing state court proceedings and 
22   does not show (or even suggest) a case of proven harassment, or a prosecution taken in bad faith.  
23   See Yelp Inc. v. Paxton, 137 F.4th 944, 951-54 (9th  Cir. 2025) (harassment must be sufficiently 
24   severe to legitimize interfering with state court proceedings in which objections could be raised; 
25   bad faith requires overwhelming lack of merit in state’s case).       
26        Because Younger abstention is appropriate as to plaintiff’s request for federal court 
27   intervention, the court may not retain jurisdiction over the request for federal court intervention 
    1    (9th Cir. 2001); Beltran v. California, 871 F.2d 777, 782 (9th Cir. 1988).  But the court need not 
    2  ||  also dismiss on abstention grounds plaintiff's claims for damages under § 1983.  See Gilbertson v.

    4  || principles apply, federal court should refrain from exercising jurisdiction temporarily and stay

    6          Unfortunately for plaintiff, his claims for damages under § 1983 against Elsworth and PDP

   11    (attorneys are private actors).  And the fact that they are acting in the same capacity as court- 
    12  || appointed public defenders does not compel a different conclusion because public defenders are 
     13  || not state actors when performing a lawyer’s traditional functions, such as entering pleas, making 
    14  || motions, objecting at trial, cross-examining witnesses, and making closing arguments.  See Polk
