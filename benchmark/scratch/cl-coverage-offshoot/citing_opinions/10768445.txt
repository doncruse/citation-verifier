13        Before the Court is Plaintiff Karla Rodrigues’s motion to remand.  Having read the parties’ 
14   papers and carefully considered their arguments and the relevant legal authority, and good cause 
15   appearing, the Court denies the motion to remand, for the following reasons. 
16                              BACKGROUND                                 
17          According to Rodrigues’s complaint, on or about July 12, 2019, Rodrigues purchased a 
18   2019 Jaguar I-Pace, which was accompanied by express and implied warranties.  Dkt. No. 1-2 
19   (“Compl.”) ¶¶ 5, 9-10.  The vehicle was delivered with “serious defects and nonconformities.”  Id. 
20   ¶ 11.  Defendant Jaguar North America, LLC has allegedly not cured the vehicle’s shortcomings, 
21   and, therefore, Rodrigues is suing for breach of express and implied warranty under the Song-
22   Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.  Id. ¶¶ 13-34.                         
23        On April 9, 2025, Jaguar was served with a summons, including a copy of the complaint, 
24   initially filed in the Superior Court of California, County of Alameda.  Dkt. No. 12-1 ¶ 7.  On June 
25   11, 2025, Jaguar received a copy of the sales contract for the vehicle.  Dkt No. 12-1 ¶ 10.  Jaguar 
26   removed the action to the Northern District of California on July 11, 2025.  Dkt. No. 1.  On August 
27   6, 2025, Rodrigues filed a motion to remand, Dkt. No. 11, which Jaguar opposed, Dkt. No. 12. 
1                            LEGAL STANDARD                               
2        District courts have original jurisdiction over civil cases where the parties are citizens of 
3   different  states  or  territories  and  the  amount  in  controversy  exceeds  $75,000.    28  U.S.C. 
4   § 1332(a)(1).  The amount in controversy is the “amount at stake in the underlying litigation,” 
5   including the payment by defendant that may result from the litigation, less interest and costs.  
6   Gonzales v. CarMax Auto Superstores, 840 F.3d 644, 648 (9th Cir. 2016) (internal citation 
7   omitted).  The amount includes all damages, the cost of complying with an injunction, and 
8   attorney’s fees under fee shifting statutes.  Id. at 648-49.          
9        A defendant must remove a case within the shorter period of (1) 30 days after receiving the 
10   initial complaint, or (2) if the pleading has been filed in court and does not need to be served upon 
11   defendant, within 30 days of receiving a summons.  28 U.S.C. § 1446(b)(1).  If a case is not 
12   removable based on the initial pleading, it may be removed within 30 days of receiving a paper 
13   from which it may be ascertained for the first time that the action is removable.  28 U.S.C. 
14   § 1446(b)(3).                                                         
15                               DISCUSSION                                
16        Rodrigues argues that remand is warranted because (1) Jaguar failed to timely remove the 
17   action, (2) Jaguar failed to establish diversity of citizenship, and (3) the principles of comity merit 
18   remand.  Dkt. No. 11.  The Court discusses each of these arguments in turn. 
19        1.   Timeliness                                                  
20        The timeliness requirements for removal are set forth in 28 U.S.C. § 1446, which provides 
21   two distinct 30-day periods in which a case may be removed to federal court.  28 U.S.C. § 1446(b).  
22   A case may be removed: (1) within thirty days after the defendant’s receipt of the initial pleading, 
23   or (2) within thirty days from when the defendant receives “an amended pleading, motion, order or 
24   other paper” that indicates a basis for removal that was not “affirmatively reveal[ed]” in the initial 
25   pleading.  Id.; Harris v. Bankers Life & Cas. Co., 425 F.3d 689, 695 (9th Cir. 2005).  If removability 
26   is unclear from the initial pleading, “the case is ‘not removable’ at that stage.”  Id. at 694.  The 
27   second thirty-day window is triggered when a case is “rendered removable by virtue of a change in 
1        Rodrigues contends that the first period applies because Jaguar “had more than adequate 
2   information to remove the case in accordance with § 1446(b)” since April 9, 2025, when Jaguar was 
3   served with a copy of the complaint.  Dkt. No. 11 at 5.  But Rodrigues did not allege a specific 
4   amount in controversy in the complaint.  Indeed, the complaint did not include the purchase price 
5   of the vehicle, a copy of the sales contract, nor any claimed amount of monetary damages.  See 
6   generally Compl.                                                      
7        It is well-settled within the Ninth Circuit that Jaguar had no obligation to conduct its own 
8   investigation or make further inquiry as to the amount in controversy.  See Harris, 425 F.3d at 694 
9   (“removability under § 1446(b) is determined through examination of the four corners of the 
10   applicable pleadings, not through subjective knowledge or a duty to make further inquiry.”); see 
11   also Garcia v. Ford Motor Co., No. 2:24-cv-00563-KJM-DB, 2024 WL 3357004, at *2 (E.D. Cal. 
12   July 9, 2024) (finding that though defendant car manufacturer could have “guessed” the amount in 
13   controversy before receipt of the sales contract, it was under no obligation to do so); McFadden v. 
14   Nissan N. Am., Inc., No. 2:23-cv-01091-AC, 2023 WL 4842332, at *3 (E.D. Cal. July 28, 2023) 
15   (same).  Thus, the sales contract was the first document Jaguar obtained from which removability 
16   could be determined.                                                  
17        Because the sales contract started the applicable thirty-day window for the purposes of 
18   removal, the sales contract constituted an “other paper” that enabled Defendant to readily determine 
19   the amount in controversy.  See 28 U.S.C. §1446(b)(3); Harris, 425 F.3d at 694.  As a result, 
20   Jaguar’s thirty-day window for removal began on June 11, 2025, when Jaguar received a copy of 
21   the sales contract.  Jaguar filed the Notice of Removal within thirty days of receiving the sales 
22   contract, so Jaguar’s removal on July 11, 2025, was timely.           
23        2.  Diversity of Citizenship                                     
24        For diversity purposes, a person is a “citizen” of the state in which they are domiciled.  See 
25   Kantor v. Wellesley Galleries, Ltd., 704 F.2d 1088, 1090 (9th Cir. 1983).  “It is a longstanding 
26   principle that ‘[t]he place where a person lives is taken to be his domicile until facts adduced 
27   establish the contrary.’”  NewGen, LLC v. Safe Cig, LLC 840 F.3d 606, 614 (9th Cir. 2016) (quoting 
1   diversity jurisdiction exists based on assertions of residence, absent evidence about domicile to the 
2   contrary.”  Ervin v. Ballard Marine Constr., Inc. No. 16-CV-02931-WHO, 2016 WL 4239710, at 
3   *3 (N.D. Cal. Aug. 11, 2016).                                         
4        Rodrigues asserts Jaguar has not adequately alleged complete diversity.  Not so.  Although 
5   residency and domicile for citizenship are not always the same, see Duran v. Allegis Glob. Sols., Inc., 
6   No. 20-CV-09025-JD, 2021 WL 3281073, at *1 (N.D. Cal. Aug. 2, 2021), the complaint avers that 
7   Rodrigues is “residing in the City of Livermore, County of Alameda, and State of California.”  
8   Compl. ¶ 1.  Rodrigues also provided a Livermore, California home address in her sales contract with 
9   Jaguar.  See Dkt. No. 12-3 at 2.  Rodrigues does not contest this, and she has provided no facts 
10   establishing that she is not domiciled in California.  Thus, the Court finds that Jaguar has adequately 
11   alleged that Rodrigues is a citizen of California.  See NewGen, 840 F.3d at 614 (9th Cir. 2016).  Also, 
12   Jaguar provides evidence that it is not a citizen of California, which Rodrigues does not contest.  
13   Therefore, diversity jurisdiction exists.                             
14        3.  Comity                                                       
15        Finally, Rodrigues asks this Court to “veto” the exercise of jurisdiction even if the Court 
16   finds that the parties are diverse.  Dkt. No. 11 at 7-9.  In support of the proposition that the Court 
17   may choose to remand the case despite the existence of diversity jurisdiction, Rodrigues cites to 
18   Grable & Sons Metal Prods., Inc. v. Darue Eng’g & Mfg., 545 U.S. 308 (2005), in which the 
19   Supreme Court considered a district court’s authority to “veto” the exercise of federal question 
20   jurisdiction where removal is based on a federal issue embedded in a state law claim.  There, the 
21   Supreme Court held that “even when the state action discloses a contested and substantial federal 
22   question, the exercise of federal jurisdiction is subject to a possible veto . . . if federal jurisdiction is 
23   consistent with congressional judgment about the sound division of labor between state and federal 
24   courts governing the application of § 1331.”  Id. at 313-14.  Nothing in Grable suggests that a district 
25   court may decline to exercise diversity jurisdiction when the removing party satisfies the statutory 
26   requirements of 28 U.S.C. § 1332.
