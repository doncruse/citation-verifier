# Legal Analysis: Did the Chicago Police Violate the First Amendment by Ordering Dispersal of a Peaceful Immigration-Protest and Arresting 30 Individuals for Failure to Disperse?

---

## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Yes, the Chicago Police Department very likely violated the First Amendment** when, on July 12, 2025, it ordered the dispersal of a 200-person peaceful protest against federal immigration policies in a downtown Chicago plaza and arrested 30 individuals for failure to comply. The conduct described — holding signs, chanting, occasional street-stepping, and verbal arguments — is quintessential core political speech in a traditional public forum. The stated justifications (traffic disruptions and "potential for escalation") do not satisfy the constitutional or statutory thresholds required under binding Seventh Circuit precedent, the Chicago Municipal Code (as narrowed by the Seventh Circuit), or foundational Supreme Court doctrine. Any resulting § 1983 claims would be strong, and officers may lack qualified immunity.

---

## II. CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK: FIRST AMENDMENT IN A TRADITIONAL PUBLIC FORUM

### A. The First Amendment Guarantee

The First Amendment provides that "Congress shall make no law … abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances." U.S. Const. amend. I. These protections apply to state and local government—including the Chicago Police—through the Fourteenth Amendment. *Edwards v. South Carolina*, 372 U.S. 229, 235 (1963).

A protest against federal immigration policy is among the most protected forms of political speech. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the First Amendment's core purpose is to protect political dissent, particularly speech directed at government policy. *NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co.*, 458 U.S. 886, 907–09 (1982) (political protest and boycotts protected even when government policy is the target).

### B. The Traditional Public Forum Doctrine

A downtown Chicago plaza is a quintessential "traditional public forum." As Justice Roberts wrote in *Hague v. Committee for Industrial Organization*, 307 U.S. 496, 515 (1939) — the foundational case establishing the public forum doctrine: "Streets and parks … have immemorially been held in trust for the use of the public and, time out of mind, have been used for purposes of assembly, communicating thoughts between citizens, and discussing public questions." In such a forum, First Amendment rights are at their zenith.

In a traditional public forum, the government faces the **highest constitutional burden** before it may restrict speech or assembly. Under the standard articulated in *Ward v. Rock Against Racism*, 491 U.S. 781, 791 (1989), and *Clark v. Community for Creative Non-Violence*, 468 U.S. 288, 293 (1984), even **content-neutral** time, place, and manner restrictions are constitutionally valid only if they are:
1. **Content-neutral** — not aimed at the message being conveyed;
2. **Narrowly tailored** to serve a **significant governmental interest**; and
3. Leave open **ample alternative channels** for communicating the information.

A content-based restriction triggers **strict scrutiny** and is presumptively unconstitutional. Even content-neutral dispersal orders must satisfy each of these three prongs. *Ward*, 491 U.S. at 791.

### C. Peaceful Protest Cannot Be Criminalized Based on Potential Disorder or Audience Reaction

The Supreme Court's decision in *Edwards v. South Carolina*, 372 U.S. 229 (1963), is strikingly on point. There, approximately 187 Black students marched peacefully to the South Carolina State House to protest discriminatory laws. A crowd of 200–300 onlookers gathered. Police, who described their own protection as "ample," ordered the students to disperse. When they refused, singing patriotic songs, all 187 were arrested for "breach of the peace." The Supreme Court unanimously reversed, holding:

> "The Fourteenth Amendment does not permit a State to make criminal the peaceful expression of unpopular views."

The Court stated that speech retains First Amendment protection even when it "induces a condition of unrest, creates dissatisfaction with conditions as they are, or even stirs people to anger." *Id.* at 237 (quoting *Terminiello v. City of Chicago*, 337 U.S. 1 (1949)). Only a **"clear and present danger of a serious substantive evil"** — one that "rises far above public inconvenience, annoyance, or unrest" — can justify suppression of otherwise protected assembly.

Similarly, in *Gregory v. City of Chicago*, 394 U.S. 111 (1969), the Supreme Court unanimously reversed disorderly conduct convictions of peaceful civil rights protesters in Chicago who marched to Mayor Daley's home, even though hostile bystanders threw rocks and eggs and police feared an imminent riot. The Court held that the demonstrators' own peaceful conduct could not form the basis of a criminal conviction. Justice Black's concurrence emphasized: "To let a policeman's command become equivalent to a criminal statute comes dangerously near making our government one of men rather than of laws." *Id.* at 125.

### D. The "Heckler's Veto" Is Constitutionally Prohibited

Police may **not** disperse a peaceful protest simply because a hostile crowd or counter-protesters react negatively. This is the forbidden "heckler's veto." *Gregory v. City of Chicago*, 394 U.S. at 125 (Black, J., concurring); *Goldhamer v. Nagode*, 621 F.3d 581 (7th Cir. 2010). The government's remedy when a crowd reacts to political speech is to control the hostile bystanders, **not** to silence the peaceful speakers.

In *Goldhamer v. Nagode*, the Seventh Circuit addressed a nearly identical situation: Chicago police issued a dispersal order to protesters peacefully leafleting near a military recruiting booth at the Taste of Chicago festival, even though no one in the vicinity was actually committing disorderly conduct. The court held that Chicago Municipal Code § 8-4-010(d) cannot be applied as a heckler's veto and that the arrests were apparently without probable cause. 621 F.3d at 588–90.

## III. ILLINOIS LAW: UNLAWFUL ASSEMBLY / MOB ACTION

### A. 720 ILCS 5/25-1 — Mob Action

Illinois does not have a traditional "unlawful assembly" statute in name. Its functional equivalent is the **Mob Action** statute, 720 ILCS 5/25-1. A person commits mob action when he or she engages in:
1. **Subsection (a)(1)**: The *knowing or reckless use of force or violence* disturbing the public peace by 2 or more persons acting together and without authority of law;
2. **Subsection (a)(2)**: The *knowing assembly of 2 or more persons with intent to commit or facilitate a felony or misdemeanor*; or
3. **Subsection (a)(3)**: Knowingly *assembling with others to resist* a peace officer performing a lawful duty.

**None of these provisions apply to the July 12, 2025 protest.** The protest was "generally peaceful." Holding signs, chanting, occasionally stepping into the street, and having verbal arguments do not constitute (1) force or violence, (2) assembly with criminal intent, or (3) resistance of a peace officer. There is no basis in 720 ILCS 5/25-1 for classifying this gathering as a mob action.

### B. 720 ILCS 5/26-1 — Illinois Disorderly Conduct

Under 720 ILCS 5/26-1(a)(1), a person commits disorderly conduct when he or she "knowingly does any act in **such unreasonable manner as to alarm or disturb another and to provoke a breach of the peace**." Courts require that the conduct both alarm others and provoke an actual, not merely speculative, breach of peace. *Thayer v. Chiczewski*, 705 F.3d 237, 248–49 (7th Cir. 2012). Verbal disagreements with passersby and occasional street-stepping fall far short of this standard. Moreover, as Illinois courts have held and the Seventh Circuit confirmed, peaceful picketing, placard-carrying, and chanting cannot form the basis of disorderly conduct charges. *Id.*

## IV. CHICAGO MUNICIPAL CODE

### A. § 10-8-330 — Parade and Public Assembly Permit Requirements

Under the Chicago Municipal Code, a **parade** — defined as a march requiring a street closing or requiring police to reroute traffic — requires a permit. A **public assembly** — an organized march or procession that is reasonably anticipated to obstruct normal pedestrian flow but does not require a full street closing — requires advance **notification** (not a permit) to the Commissioner of Transportation. § 10-8-330(a)(1)–(2).

For **spontaneous assemblies** (those that cannot practicably provide five-business-day advance notice), notification should be provided "as soon as practicable." § 10-8-330(r). Critically, a *sidewalk gathering* — where participants largely stay on sidewalks — may require **neither a permit nor formal notification**. In *Thayer v. Chiczewski*, 705 F.3d at 247, both senior police officers testified under oath that a sidewalk press conference requires no permit at all.

Here, the 200 protesters "mostly stayed on the sidewalk" and "occasionally stepped into the street." This conduct is consistent with an unpermitted sidewalk assembly or, at most, a public assembly requiring only notification — not the formal permit that a full street-closing parade would demand. The absence of a permit alone does not render the assembly unlawful.

### B. § 8-4-010(d) — Failure to Disperse (Disorderly Conduct) and Its Constitutional Limitations

Chicago Municipal Code § 8-4-010(d) makes it a criminal offense when a person knowingly:

> "Fails to obey a lawful order of dispersal by a person known by him to be a peace officer under circumstances where **three or more persons are committing acts of disorderly conduct** in the immediate vicinity, which acts are likely to cause **substantial harm** or serious inconvenience, annoyance or alarm."

However, the Seventh Circuit in *Bell v. Keating*, 697 F.3d 445 (7th Cir. 2012), **partially struck down § 8-4-010(d)** as facially overbroad and unconstitutionally vague. The court:
- **Severed and invalidated** the "serious inconvenience, annoyance or alarm" triggers;
- **Upheld only** the "substantial harm" trigger as constitutionally valid.

The court held that "avoiding annoyance is never a proper basis on which to curtail protected speech," citing *Coates v. City of Cincinnati*, 402 U.S. 611 (1971). *Bell v. Keating*, 697 F.3d at 454. The "alarm" and "annoyance" terms were struck as both overbroad (encumbering substantially more constitutionally protected expression than legitimate conduct) and unconstitutionally vague (providing officers with standardless discretion and failing to give citizens adequate notice). *Id.* at 455–58.

**As of July 12, 2025, a constitutionally valid dispersal order under § 8-4-010(d) requires that three or more persons in the immediate vicinity be committing acts of disorderly conduct likely to cause *substantial harm* — i.e., imminent physical danger or significant property damage.** Neither verbal arguments with passersby nor occasional street-stepping approaches this threshold.

## V. ANALYSIS OF THE DISPERSAL ORDER

### A. The Stated Justifications Are Constitutionally Insufficient

The Chicago Police issued the dispersal order based on two stated concerns:
1. **Traffic disruptions**; and
2. **Potential for escalation**.

**Traffic Disruption:**

Occasional stepping into the street by some protesters in a 200-person march is a typical and incidental feature of sidewalk demonstrations in urban settings. It does not constitute a "substantial harm" triggering lawful dispersal under the narrowed § 8-4-010(d). Even in *Vodak v. City of Chicago*, 639 F.3d 738, 744 (7th Cir. 2011) — where the court upheld the *authority* to issue a dispersal order — the justification involved 8,000 protesters occupying major arterial roadways, some marchers banging on cars in rush-hour traffic, and shouts suggesting a threat to a commercial district. The Seventh Circuit described that as a march that was "getting out of hand." *Id.* No such evidence exists here.

Moreover, the Supreme Court confirmed in *Cox v. Louisiana*, 379 U.S. 536, 554–55 (1965), that even where traffic regulations are validly implicated, the government cannot use them as a pretext to suppress protected political expression. And as the Seventh Circuit recognized in *Thayer v. Chiczewski*, 705 F.3d at 246, Chicago officers may regulate specific individual acts of traffic obstruction without issuing a blanket dispersal order against an entire assembly.

**Potential for Escalation:**

The "potential for escalation" is precisely the speculative, subjective concern that *Edwards v. South Carolina* and *Gregory v. City of Chicago* held to be constitutionally insufficient. As the Supreme Court made clear in *Edwards*, police protection is "ample," a watching crowd is not itself threatening, and subjective police concern that a situation *might* deteriorate does not supply the clear and present danger of serious substantive harm needed to justify dispersing a peaceful assembly. 372 U.S. at 236. The Court drew a careful distinction between *Feiner v. New York*, 340 U.S. 315 (1951) — where a speaker had genuinely incited near-riot conditions, the crowd was surging, and police authority was being actively challenged — and an ordinary peaceful protest attended by verbal disagreements. Here, the facts align far more closely with *Edwards* and *Gregory* than with *Feiner*.

**Verbal Arguments:**

"A couple of verbal arguments with people passing by" is protected expressive activity. The Supreme Court has held that speech "may best serve its high purpose when it induces a condition of unrest, creates dissatisfaction with conditions as they are, or even stirs people to anger." *Edwards*, 372 U.S. at 237. Verbal disagreement is not a "fighting words" situation under *Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire*, 315 U.S. 568 (1942), absent actual, face-to-face provocations of an immediate breach of the peace. Nothing in the record suggests this threshold was crossed.

### B. Failure to Satisfy the § 8-4-010(d) Predicate

For a dispersal order to be lawful under § 8-4-010(d) (as constitutionally narrowed by *Bell v. Keating*), **three or more persons in the immediate vicinity must actually be committing disorderly conduct likely to cause substantial harm**. The conduct here — peaceful political protest with minor, incidental traffic intrusion and verbal debates — does not satisfy this standard. The Seventh Circuit in *Goldhamer v. Nagode* found that when "the police appear to have had no arguable probable cause" based on the predicate disorderly conduct, arrests for failure to disperse are actionable under § 1983. 621 F.3d at 591.

### C. The Dispersal Order as a Content-Neutral Time, Place, and Manner Restriction

Even analyzing the dispersal order as a content-neutral time, place, and manner restriction under *Ward v. Rock Against Racism*, 491 U.S. 781, it must be **narrowly tailored** to serve a **significant governmental interest** and must **leave open ample alternative channels**. A blanket order telling 200 protesters to disperse entirely — not to move to a different location, not to reduce their footprint, but to disband altogether — is almost certainly not "narrowly tailored." A narrowly tailored response to minor traffic disruption would be to direct individual protesters blocking the street to move to the sidewalk, not to terminate the entire lawful assembly.

The Supreme Court in *Brandenburg v. Ohio*, 395 U.S. 444, 447 (1969), further established that advocacy of general ideas — including advocacy against government immigration policy — retains full First Amendment protection unless it is "*directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action* and is *likely* to incite or produce such action." Chanting slogans and holding signs challenging immigration policy, even loudly and emphatically, does not satisfy this standard.

## VI. ANALYSIS OF THE ARRESTS FOR FAILURE TO DISPERSE

Even if the dispersal order itself were conceded to be lawful (a contested point under the above analysis), the **arrests of 30 individuals** raise independent constitutional concerns.

### A. The Notice Requirement — Cox v. Louisiana and Vodak

The Supreme Court established in *Cox v. Louisiana*, 379 U.S. 571, 571–73 (1965), that police **must give notice** of a dispersal order and must **not begin arresting demonstrators** until that notice has been communicated. This principle is binding and long-established.

The Seventh Circuit in *Vodak v. City of Chicago*, 639 F.3d at 747, expressly applied this rule: "Before the police could start arresting peaceable demonstrators for defying their orders, they had to communicate the orders to the demonstrators." The court further held that arrests are unlawful where there is no "good reason to believe that the protesters who were arrested, or at least most of them, had heard the orders." *Id.* at 748.

Here, the order was "issued over a loudspeaker." For 200 protesters in a downtown plaza, this raises legitimate questions: Could all 200 hear it? Were the acoustics adequate? Were protesters positioned throughout the area such that those at the periphery might not have received the order? Were foreign-language speakers adequately notified? These factual questions are material to whether the requisite notice was given.

### B. The Individual Probable-Cause Requirement

Even where a dispersal order is valid, police must have **individualized probable cause** to believe each arrested person (1) heard the dispersal order, (2) understood it, and (3) willfully defied it. *Vodak*, 639 F.3d at 748; *Barham v. Ramsey*, 434 F.3d 565, 573 (D.C. Cir. 2006). The fact that some individuals in a group may have been disorderly does not supply probable cause to arrest all individuals, including those who were peaceably assembled and had not necessarily received the order. *Vodak*, 639 F.3d at 748–49 ("The fact that some of the marchers were rowdy and may have committed criminal acts … need not be discussed in detail; demonstrators' misconduct cannot … be thought to have justified the mass arrests of [others].").

Arresting 30 people based on a general dispersal order — without individualized assessments of who heard the order, who was actually disorderly (if anyone), and who made a knowing choice to remain — risks the same constitutional defects the Seventh Circuit condemned in *Vodak*: the government cannot entrap citizens "by convicting a citizen for exercising a privilege which the State had clearly told him was available to him." *Id.* at 746 (quoting *Cox v. Louisiana*, 379 U.S. at 571).

### C. The Anti-Entrapment Principle

Protesters who were assembled lawfully — on a sidewalk, chanting, holding signs — were exercising a privilege the Constitution makes available. When police issued the dispersal order based on insufficient grounds, then arrested 30 individuals who may not have fully understood that their lawful activity had been reclassified as unlawful, the constitutional concern is acute. As the Seventh Circuit noted in *Vodak*, it would be "an indefensible sort of entrapment by the State" to first allow lawful conduct, then issue a dispersal order, and immediately arrest those who did not comply. 639 F.3d at 746.

## VII. CIVIL RIGHTS LIABILITY AND QUALIFIED IMMUNITY CONSIDERATIONS

### A. Section 1983 Claims

The arrested individuals have strong § 1983 claims under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 against both the officers and the City of Chicago for:
- **First Amendment violations** (retaliatory or unconstitutionally based dispersal order);
- **Fourth Amendment violations** (unlawful seizure/arrest without probable cause).

The Seventh Circuit's guidance in *Vodak* suggests that when, as here, no valid predicate exists for a dispersal order and arrests follow without adequate notice, both individual officers and the municipality (through final policymaking authority under *Monell v. Dep't of Soc. Servs.*, 436 U.S. 658 (1978)) may be liable.

### B. Qualified Immunity

Officers asserting qualified immunity would argue no "clearly established" law was violated. However, *Bell v. Keating* (2012), *Vodak* (2011), *Goldhamer* (2010), *Thayer* (2012), *Edwards* (1963), *Gregory* (1969), and *Cox* (1965) have collectively established for decades that:
- Peaceful political assemblies in public forums are protected;
- Dispersal orders require actual, not speculative, disorderly conduct;
- Notice must precede arrests;
- Individual probable cause is required for each arrest.

These principles were sufficiently "clearly established" by July 12, 2025, to defeat qualified immunity in most of these circumstances, particularly given the Seventh Circuit's 2011 and 2012 precedents squarely addressing Chicago protest law. *See Vodak*, 639 F.3d at 750 (*Cox v. Louisiana* established notice requirement "decades" before 2003, meaning qualified immunity was unavailable then — a fortiori by 2025).

---

## VIII. CONCLUSION

**The Chicago Police Department likely violated the First Amendment when it ordered dispersal of the July 12, 2025 peaceful immigration-policy protest and arrested 30 individuals for failure to comply.** The specific grounds for this conclusion are:

1. **Protected Core Speech in a Traditional Public Forum**: A 200-person political protest against federal immigration policy in a downtown Chicago plaza is precisely what the First Amendment's Assembly Clause was designed to protect. It sits at the zenith of constitutional protection under *Hague v. CIO*, *Edwards v. South Carolina*, and *Gregory v. City of Chicago*.

2. **The Dispersal Order Lacked a Lawful Predicate**: Under Chicago Municipal Code § 8-4-010(d), as narrowed by *Bell v. Keating*, 697 F.3d 445 (7th Cir. 2012), a lawful dispersal order requires three or more persons in the immediate vicinity committing disorderly conduct likely to cause **substantial harm**. Minor, incidental traffic-stepping and verbal arguments do not satisfy this standard. The "potential for escalation" justification is precisely the type of speculative risk that *Edwards v. South Carolina* held is constitutionally insufficient.

3. **The Dispersal Order Failed Time, Place, and Manner Standards**: Even as a content-neutral restriction, the blanket order to disperse a 200-person peaceful protest entirely — rather than narrowly targeting individual acts of actual obstruction — was not narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest under *Ward v. Rock Against Racism*, 491 U.S. 781.

4. **The Arrests for Failure to Disperse Were Constitutionally Defective**: Under *Cox v. Louisiana*, 379 U.S. 536, and *Vodak v. City of Chicago*, 639 F.3d 738, police must clearly communicate a dispersal order to each individual and must have individualized probable cause to believe each arrestee heard and willfully defied the order. Arresting 30 individuals from a 200-person crowd without these determinations is constitutionally infirm.

5. **No Evidence of "Imminent Lawless Action"**: The *Brandenburg v. Ohio*, 395 U.S. 444 (1969), standard — requiring speech directed to and likely to produce *imminent* lawless action — was nowhere close to being met on these facts. The Illinois Mob Action statute, 720 ILCS 5/25-1, similarly requires actual force/violence or criminal intent, neither of which was present.

The individuals arrested have viable § 1983 claims for First and Fourth Amendment violations, and officers may be denied qualified immunity given the decades-old, clearly established nature of these principles in both Supreme Court precedent and Seventh Circuit law.
