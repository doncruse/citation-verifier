Actual language in opinion:

Coates squarely supports the narrower proposition that avoiding annoyance is not a proper basis for curtailing protected speech or assembly. The Court struck down a Cincinnati ordinance criminalizing conduct “annoying to persons passing by” as both unconstitutionally vague and overbroad, holding that the First and Fourteenth Amendments do not permit a State to make criminal the exercise of the right of assembly simply because its exercise may be annoying to some people, and that mere public intolerance or animosity cannot be the basis for abridgment of these freedoms.

The substance is therefore well grounded in the cited opinion. The brief's framing is accurate in attributing the underlying principle to Coates, but the quoted phrase is a reworded paraphrase rather than Coates's own text.

p. 1 — Barham v. Ramsey, 434 F.3d 565, 573 (D.C. Cir. 2006) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Even where a dispersal order is valid, police must have individualized probable cause to believe each arrested person (1) heard the dispersal order, (2) understood it, and (3) willfully defied it. Vodak, 639 F.3d at 748; Barham v. Ramsey, 434 F.3d 565, 573 (D.C. Cir. 2006).

Actual language in opinion:

Dellums held that a group of demonstrators lawfully gathered on the Capitol steps could not be arrested unless the Chief had reason to believe (1) that the demonstrators could be validly evicted under the Capitol Grounds ordinance, (2) that the police gave demonstrators an order to disperse that “apprised the crowd as a whole that it was under an obligation to leave,” and (3) that there had been a “reasonable opportunity” to comply. ... the “fair notice” required by the Cullinane court is notice reasonably likely to have reached all of the crowd despite any noise the demonstrators may have been making.

Barham v. Ramsey is a Fourth Amendment qualified-immunity case about a mass arrest of anti-globalization demonstrators in Pershing Park. It addresses the brief's general topic, holding that an officer cannot sweep up an “undifferentiated mass” of people absent probable cause “particularized with respect to” each person, and that police may arrest a crowd as a unit only after issuing a valid dispersal order with “fair notice” reasonably likely to reach the whole crowd and a reasonable opportunity to comply.

The gap is that the brief recasts those principles into a specific three-element test — individualized probable cause that each arrestee “heard,” “understood,” and “willfully defied” the order — that the opinion never articulates. Barham requires particularized probable cause for the offense of arrest and, separately, a valid order plus fair notice and opportunity to comply; it does not frame the notice-and-defiance elements as components of “individualized probable cause,” and “understood it” in particular has no counterpart in the text. Because the Pershing Park officers issued no dispersal order at all, the court never reached the question of what probable cause is needed to believe a particular person heard and defied an order. The proposition is a reasonable extension of the dispersal-order and particularized-probable-cause holdings, but it overstates and over-formalizes what the case actually decided.

p. 1 — Ward v. Rock Against Racism, 491 U.S. 781 (1989) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Even as a content-neutral restriction, the blanket order to disperse a 200-person peaceful protest entirely — rather than narrowly targeting individual acts of actual obstruction — was not narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest under Ward v. Rock Against Racism, 491 U.S. 781.

Actual language in opinion:

a regulation of the time, place, or manner of protected speech must be narrowly tailored to serve the government's legitimate, content-neutral interests but that it need not be the least restrictive or least intrusive means of doing so.

Ward supplies the narrow-tailoring standard the brief invokes, but it does not hold that a blanket dispersal order fails that standard, and its central holding cuts partly against the brief's framing. Ward concerns a municipal sound-amplification guideline, not crowd dispersal, and the Court expressly rejected the "least intrusive means" requirement, holding that a content-neutral regulation "need not be the least restrictive or least intrusive means" of serving the government's interest. The brief's argument that officers should have "narrowly target[ed] individual acts" instead of dispersing the whole assembly is a less-restrictive-alternative argument of the kind Ward disapproved.

Ward does recognize the outer limit the brief gestures toward — a regulation may not "burden substantially more speech than is necessary" to advance the government's goals — so the narrow-tailoring standard is correctly stated. But the specific conclusion that a blanket dispersal order flunks narrow tailoring is the brief's own extension and application, not a holding of Ward.

Methodology & Limitations

  • Quote verification is near-deterministic but limited by the quality of CourtListener's text. Minor discrepancies may reflect OCR artifacts in the source database rather than errors in the brief.
  • Propositional support assessment is an AI judgment. It reflects Claude's reading of the opinion text and should be verified by counsel. Reasonable lawyers may disagree about whether a case "supports" a given proposition.
  • This report checks whether cited cases say what the brief claims they say. It does not assess the overall legal merit of the brief's arguments or whether the cited cases are the best authorities available.
  • Cases marked "unable to verify" should be checked through other sources (Westlaw, Lexis, etc.).
