p. 48 — Nix v. Whiteside, 475 U.S. 157, 166 (1986). — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Courts have consistently recognized that the purpose of the rules governing conflicts of interest is not only to protect the interests of clients but also to maintain the integrity of the judicial process.

Actual language in opinion:

an attorney's ethical duty to advance the interests of his client is limited by an equally solemn duty to comply with the law and standards of professional conduct; it specifically ensures that the client may not use false evidence.

Nix v. Whiteside is a Sixth Amendment ineffective-assistance case about whether counsel violates the right to counsel by refusing to help a client present perjured testimony; its subject is the attorney's duty of candor to the tribunal, not the rules governing conflicts of interest. The opinion genuinely supports the second half of the proposition — that an attorney's obligations run not only to the client but also to the integrity of the judicial process — describing the lawyer as "an officer of the court and a key component of a system of justice, dedicated to a search for truth" and holding that the duty to advance client interests is "limited by an equally solemn duty to comply with the law and standards of professional conduct."

But the Court expressly distinguished the client-perjury "conflict" before it from a true conflict of interest, stating that "this is not remotely the kind of conflict of interests dealt with in Cuyler v. Sullivan." The brief thus recharacterizes a candor/perjury holding as a statement about the purpose of conflict-of-interest rules, a principle Nix does not articulate. The pinpoint at page 166 falls in the passage where the Court declines to define the relative weight of ethics canons and the Sixth Amendment because "virtually all of the sources speak with one voice" — language that does not state the cited proposition.

p. 65 — Midwest Employers Cas. Co. v. Williams, 161 F.3d 877, 879 (5th Cir. 1998). — Quote not found in opinion
What the brief claims:

The Fifth Circuit has consistently held that "the excusable neglect standard is a strict one" and that "inadvertence, ignorance of the rules, or mistakes construing the rules do not usually constitute excusable neglect."

Actual language in opinion:

Where, as here, the rule at issue is unambiguous, a district court's determination that the neglect was inexcusable is virtually unassailable. [...] The nature of this very mistake "weighs heavily against a finding of excusable neglect."

The opinion substantively supports the proposition: it holds that counsel's misreading of an unambiguous procedural rule (Rule 6(e)) does not constitute excusable neglect under Appellate Rule 4(a)(5), describing such a determination as "virtually unassailable" and the mistake as weighing "heavily against a finding of excusable neglect," while leaving open only the "rare case" in which a rule misinterpretation might qualify. The core principle the brief draws — that the standard is applied strictly and that mistakes construing the rules usually do not amount to excusable neglect — tracks the opinion's reasoning and its reversal of the magistrate's extension.

Neither quoted string, however, appears anywhere in the opinion. The language "inadvertence, ignorance of the rules, or mistakes construing the rules do not usually constitute excusable neglect" is the Supreme Court's formulation from Pioneer, and this opinion quotes only Pioneer's equitable-factors passage, not that line; the phrase "the excusable neglect standard is a strict one" likewise does not appear. The substance is sound, but the brief presents fabricated quotations as though they were drawn from this case.

p. 65 — Silvercreek Mgmt., Inc. v. Banc of Am. Sec., LLC, 534 F.3d 469, 472 (5th Cir. 2008). — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

The Fifth Circuit has consistently held that delays within a party's control do not constitute excusable neglect.

Actual language in opinion:

The district court did not abuse its discretion in refusing to extend the opt-out date, because it considered Silvercreek's proffered evidence and determined that counsel's performance fell below the threshold required for neglect to be excusable.

Silvercreek is directly on point as to excusable neglect: applying the Pioneer factors, the Fifth Circuit affirmed the district court's refusal to extend an opt-out deadline because the delay was attributable to counsel's own failure to track a deadline it had notice of — a delay squarely within the party's control. That substance lends real support to the brief's proposition.

The brief nonetheless overstates the holding. This is a single decision reviewed for abuse of discretion and resting on case-specific facts (counsel received the order, should have noticed the blank opt-out date, and could have inquired), not a categorical rule that the court has "consistently held." Indeed, the opinion cuts against treating excusable neglect as mechanical: it notes that "Silvercreek incorrectly contends that the district court must rigorously apply each of these factors in every case," emphasizing a discretionary, multi-factor analysis. Recasting that fact-bound, deferential ruling as a consistently-held categorical principle extends the case beyond what it decided.

p. 72 — American Auto. Ass'n v. AAA Legal Clinic of Jefferson Crooke, 930 F.2d 1117, 1120 (5th Cir. 1991). — Reworded -- not a verbatim quote
What the brief claims:

Deemed admissions are "judicial admissions" that are binding on the party and cannot be contradicted at trial or on a motion for summary judgment.

Actual language in opinion:

In form and substance a Rule 36 admission is comparable to an admission in pleadings or a stipulation drafted by counsel for use at trial, rather than to an evidentiary admission of a party.... An admission that is not withdrawn or amended cannot be rebutted by contrary testimony or ignored by the district court simply because it finds the evidence presented by the party against whom the admission operates more credible.... The opinion substantively supports the proposition: it holds that Rule 36 admissions are "conclusively established," that an admission "cannot be rebutted by contrary testimony or ignored by the district court," and that this conclusive effect "applies equally to those admissions made affirmatively and those established by default" -- i.e., deemed admissions bind the party and cannot be contradicted. The opinion's characterization that such an admission is "comparable to an admission in pleadings or a stipulation... rather than to an evidentiary admission of a party" is the substance behind labeling them "judicial admissions."

Two caveats keep this short of a clean quote match. The opinion never uses the literal phrase "judicial admissions" (hence the close/reworded quote), and because the case arose after a completed bench trial it does not itself mention summary judgment -- though the conclusive-effect language it states is general and not trial-specific, so the brief's reference to summary judgment is a routine application rather than a misstatement.

p. 105 — Stringer v. Trapp, 30 So. 3d 339, 342 (Miss. 2010). — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Under Mississippi law, the question of when a plaintiff should have discovered an injury through the exercise of reasonable diligence is an issue of fact to be decided by a jury.

Actual language in opinion:

[O]ccasionally the question of whether the suit is barred by the statute of limitations is a question of fact for the jury; however, as with other putative fact questions, the question may be taken away from the jury if reasonable minds could not differ as to the conclusion.

Stringer does address whether the limitations/discovery question is a jury issue: it observes that the discovery-rule inquiry "often turns on the factual determination of 'what the plaintiff knew and when,'" and that the limitations question can be "a question of fact for the jury." But the opinion frames this as a conditional, not the categorical rule the brief asserts. The court says the question is "[o]ccasionally" one for the jury, and expressly adds that "the question may be taken away from the jury if reasonable minds could not differ as to the conclusion" — which is exactly how summary judgment can still be entered on limitations grounds.

The brief drops those qualifiers and presents the proposition as an unconditional rule that the reasonable-diligence/discovery question "is an issue of fact to be decided by a jury." That overstates the holding; the opinion supports the topic but expressly preserves the court's power to resolve the issue as a matter of law when reasonable minds could not differ, making this partial rather than full support.

p. 105 — Young v. S. Farm Bureau Life Ins. Co., 592 So. 2d 103, 107 (Miss. 1991). — Reworded -- not a verbatim quote
What the brief claims:

The statute of limitations for a breach of contract claim begins to run at the time of the breach.

Actual language in opinion:

[i]n the case of a breach of contract, the cause of action accrues at the time of the breach... .

The opinion substantively supports the proposition. Methodology & Limitations
