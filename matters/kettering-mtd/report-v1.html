p. 2 — Heskett v. Van Horn Title Agency, Inc., 2006-Ohio-6900, ¶ 26 (10th Dist.) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
Ohio does not recognize a civil cause of action for “extortion.” See Simpson v. Voiture Nationale La Société des Quarante Hommes et Huit Chevaux, 2021-Ohio-2131, ¶¶ 13-15 (2d Dist.) (affirming dismissal; “Ohio law does not recognize a civil action for extortion”), citing First Fed. Bank of Ohio v. Angelini, 2012-Ohio-2136, ¶ 6 (3d Dist.), and Heskett v. Van Horn Title Agency, Inc., 2006-Ohio-6900, ¶ 26 (10th Dist.).

The opinion addresses summary judgment on breach of contract, negligence, fraud, and defamation claims arising from a deed error and never discusses extortion (it only notes coercion is not a known civil cause of action), so it does not support the proposition and the quote check is FABRICATED.

p. 2 — Office Depot, Inc. v. Impact Off. Prods., LLC, 821 F. Supp. 2d 912, 919-23 (N.D. Ohio 2011) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Further Kettering fails to identify any trade secret with particularity or reasonable secrecy measures. See 18 U.S.C. § 1839(5); In re Protech Indus., 51 F.4th 714, 720-22 (6th Cir. 2022) (reasonable measures); Office Depot, Inc. v. Impact Off. Prods., LLC, 821 F. Supp. 2d 912, 919-23 (N.D. Ohio 2011) (particularity and OUTSA preemption).

The opinion squarely supports the OUTSA-preemption half of the proposition but never addresses any trade-secret-particularity requirement, so the conjunctive proposition overstates what this case holds.

p. 2 — Wilson v. Collins, 517 F.3d 421, 429 (6th Cir. 2008) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
HIPAA provides no private right of action, and the materials at issue are de-identified by subject codes, not “PHI.” See Wilson v. Collins, 517 F.3d 421, 429 (6th Cir. 2008); 45 C.F.R. § 164.514(b)(2).

Wilson v. Collins is a constitutional challenge to Ohio's DNA collection statute (Fourth/Fifth Amendment and due process) and says nothing about HIPAA or any HIPAA private right of action, so it addresses a completely different topic and does not support the proposition.

p. 3 — First Fed. Bank of Ohio v. Angelini, 2012-Ohio-2136, ¶ 6 (3d Dist.) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Ohio courts have repeatedly held there is no stand-alone civil cause of action for extortion. Simpson, 2021-Ohio-2131, ¶¶ 13-15; Angelini, 2012-Ohio-2136, ¶ 6; Heskett, 2006-Ohio-6900, ¶ 26.

The opinion holds at ¶ 14 that "Ohio law does not recognize a civil action for extortion," supporting the core point, but as a single Third District decision it does not itself show Ohio courts have "repeatedly" so held, mildly overstating the rule.

p. 3 — Heskett v. Van Horn Title Agency, Inc., 2006-Ohio-6900, ¶ 26 (10th Dist.) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
Ohio courts have repeatedly held there is no stand-alone civil cause of action for extortion. Simpson, 2021-Ohio-2131, ¶¶ 13-15; Angelini, 2012-Ohio-2136, ¶ 6; Heskett, 2006-Ohio-6900, ¶ 26.

The opinion holds only that "coercion" is not a cognizable civil cause of action (¶ 26); it never addresses or mentions extortion, so it does not support the proposition that Ohio courts have repeatedly held there is no stand-alone civil cause of action for extortion.

p. 4 — United States v. Pendergraft, 297 F.3d 1198, 1205 (11th Cir. 2002) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Mere “hard bargaining,” even when the consequences are severe, is not extortion where claims are legitimate and conduct threatened is lawful. See United States v. Pendergraft, 297 F.3d 1198, 1205 (11th Cir. 2002).

Pendergraft supports the general idea that a bad-faith threat to litigate (even with false affidavits) is not Hobbs Act extortion, but it does not turn on the cited frame of legitimate claims or a damages element, the proposition overstates its scope, and the FABRICATED quote check requires at least a Yellow downgrade.

p. 5 — United States v. Jackson, 180 F.3d 55, 70 (2d Cir. 1999) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
“Informing a party that you will report their misconduct to appropriate authorities if corrective action is not taken does not constitute an unlawful threat.” United States v. Jackson, 180 F.3d 55, 70 (2d Cir. 1999). Thus, advising Kettering of Ms. Collier's statutory obligations cannot constitute extortion.

Jackson holds a reputation-injury threat is extortion under 18 U.S.C. 875(d) when the threatener has no plausible claim of right (here, threatening tabloid disclosure of paternity to extort money from Cosby); it does not support the proposition that informing a party you will report misconduct to authorities is lawful, and the supporting quote is fabricated.

p. 5 — Flatley v. Mauro, 39 Cal.4th 299, 331 (2006) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
“Seeking a monetary settlement tied directly to legal claims does not become extortion merely because regulatory reporting is also contemplated.” — Flatley v. Mauro, 39 Cal.4th 299, 331 (2006).

Flatley held the opposite: a monetary settlement demand coupled with threats to report misconduct to authorities and publicize allegations constituted criminal extortion as a matter of law, and the quoted language is fabricated.

p. 6 — Kenty v. Transamerica Premium Ins. Co., 72 Ohio St.3d 415, 419 (1995) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
“Where claims are based on enforceable legal rights, settlement demands are privileged.” — Kenty, 72 Ohio St.3d at 419.

Kenty addresses tortious interference with a contract and civil conspiracy in an insurance-overcharge dispute and says nothing about extortion, settlement demands, or privilege for demands based on enforceable legal rights, and the quote check is FABRICATED.

p. 6 — United States v. Pendergraft, 297 F.3d 1198, 1205 (11th Cir. 2002) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
“Advising of possible consequences from lawful reporting cannot form the basis of an extortion claim.” — United States v. Pendergraft, 297 F.3d at 1205.

Pendergraft holds that a bad-faith threat to sue a government, even with fabricated affidavits, is not wrongful under the Hobbs Act; it does not address advising a party of consequences from lawful reporting to authorities, so it only partially fits the proposition, and the quote check is FABRICATED.

p. 7 — Kulch v. Structural Fibers, Inc., 78 Ohio St.3d 134, 150 (1997) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Reporting misconduct to regulators and sponsors is protected conduct: Whistleblower Protection Act, 5 U.S.C. § 2302; False Claims Act retaliation provisions, 31 U.S.C. § 3730(h); Ohio's public policy protections (Kulch v. Structural Fibers, Inc., 78 Ohio St.3d 134, 150 (1997)).

Kulch supports that Ohio public policy protects an at-will employee's reporting of workplace violations to a regulator (OSHA) via a wrongful-discharge tort, but the proposition overstates and generalizes the holding (e.g., adds reporting to "sponsors," which the case never addresses, and omits the statute's strict-compliance limits).

p. 7 — Flatley v. Mauro, 39 Cal.4th 299, 330 (2006) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
Courts consistently hold that truthful disclosure of misconduct does not constitute extortion. Flatley, 39 Cal.4th at 330; Jackson, 180 F.3d at 70.

Flatley holds the opposite of the proposition: it found that Mauro's threats to expose/report Flatley's alleged misconduct unless paid constituted criminal extortion as a matter of law, and that such threats are extortionate even when the victim actually committed the crime or indiscretion.

p. 7 — United States v. Jackson, 180 F.3d 55, 70 (2d Cir. 1999) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
Courts consistently hold that truthful disclosure of misconduct does not constitute extortion. Flatley, 39 Cal.4th at 330; Jackson, 180 F.3d at 70.

Jackson holds the opposite: under 18 U.S.C. 875(d) the truth of the damaging allegations is not a defense, and even a truthful threatened disclosure is extortion when it lacks a nexus to a plausible claim of right, so the case does not support and contradicts the proposition that truthful disclosure of misconduct cannot be extortion.

p. 8 — Office Depot, Inc. v. Impact Off. Prods., LLC, 821 F. Supp. 2d 912, 919-23 (N.D. Ohio 2011) — Not supported by cited case
What the brief claims:
The Complaint identifies only broad categories of information “clinical data” and “research processes”, which do not satisfy the particularity requirement. Office Depot, 821 F. Supp. 2d at 919-23.

The opinion addresses only OUTSA preemption of common-law claims under the Allied same-facts standard and never discusses a trade-secret particularity/identification requirement, so it does not support the proposition (and the quote check was FABRICATED).

p. 9 — Office Depot, Inc. v. Impact Off. Prods., LLC, 821 F. Supp. 2d 912, 918-23 (N.D. Ohio 2011) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Its tortious-interference and conversion theories are preempted by OUTSA where the alleged harm stems from misappropriation. Ohio Rev. Code § 1333.67(A); Office Depot, 821 F. Supp. 2d at 918-23.

The opinion supports OUTSA preemption of tortious-interference and conversion/unjust-enrichment theories only under a partial 'same facts' standard, preempting just the portions resting on misappropriation while expressly preserving claims with an independent factual basis, so the proposition's categorical framing overstates the qualified holding.

p. 9 — Hecht v. Levin, 66 Ohio St. 3d 458, 460-61, 613 N.E.2d 585 (1993) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:
Any defamation-based claim arising from litigation conduct is absolutely privileged. Hecht, 66 Ohio St. 3d at 460-61.

Hecht v. Levin holds litigation-related defamatory statements are absolutely privileged only when they bear some reasonable relation to the proceeding, so the unqualified "any defamation-based claim" framing overstates the holding by omitting that relevance limitation.

Methodology & Limitations

  • Quote verification is near-deterministic but limited by the quality of CourtListener's text. Minor discrepancies may reflect OCR artifacts in the source database rather than errors in the brief.
  • Propositional support assessment is an AI judgment. It reflects Claude's reading of the opinion text and should be verified by counsel. Reasonable lawyers may disagree about whether a case "supports" a given proposition.
  • This report checks whether cited cases say what the brief claims they say. It does not assess the overall legal merit of the brief's arguments or whether the cited cases are the best authorities available.
  • Cases marked "unable to verify" should be checked through other sources (Westlaw, Lexis, etc.).
