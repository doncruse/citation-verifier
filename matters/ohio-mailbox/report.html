p. 20 — Marchetti v. Kalish, 53 Ohio St. 3d 95 (1990) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

Primary assumption of risk remains conceptually distinct in contexts where the defendant owes no duty as to risks inherent in the activity, as reflected in Ohio cases such as Marchetti v. Kalish, 53 Ohio St. 3d 95 (1990).

Actual language in opinion:

We hold that where individuals engage in recreational or sports activities, they assume the ordinary risks of the activity and cannot recover for any injury unless it can be shown that the other participant's actions were either “reckless” or “intentional” as defined in Sections 500 and 8A of the Restatement of Torts 2d.

Marchetti v. Kalish holds that participants in recreational or sporting activities "assume the ordinary risks of the activity" and cannot recover absent reckless or intentional conduct, so the opinion is squarely on the topic of assumption of risk in sports and supports the general idea that participants accept inherent risks. The opinion does not, however, articulate the modern doctrinal vocabulary the brief invokes -- it never uses the term "primary assumption of risk," never frames the rule as one where the defendant "owes no duty," and never distinguishes primary from secondary assumption of risk. Marchetti instead grounds its rule in the conduct of the tortfeasor (reckless or intentional), declining to adopt a consent-based Restatement analysis. The proposition that primary assumption of risk "remains conceptually distinct" and operates through a "no duty" framing extends the holding into a doctrinal taxonomy the opinion itself does not develop, so the support is partial rather than direct.
