p. — Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. G.W. Thomas Drayage & Rigging Co., 69 Cal.2d 33 (1968) — Overstated -- case partially supports
What the brief claims:

In Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. G.W. Thomas Drayage & Rigging Co., 69 Cal.2d 33 (1968), the California Supreme Court held that extrinsic evidence—including expert testimony—may be admitted to explain the meaning of contract terms, even if the language appears clear on its face.

Actual language in opinion:

The test of admissibility of extrinsic evidence to explain the meaning of a written instrument is not whether it appears to the court to be plain and unambiguous on its face, but whether the offered evidence is relevant to prove a meaning to which the language of the instrument is reasonably susceptible.

The core of the brief's proposition is well supported: Traustein's opinion squarely holds that extrinsic evidence to explain a written instrument is admissible even when the language seems plain on its face, turning instead on whether the language is reasonably susceptible of the proffered meaning. The gap is the parenthetical "including expert testimony." The opinion's extrinsic-evidence holding concerns admissions of the parties, course of dealing, surrounding circumstances, and trade usage—not expert testimony on contract meaning. The only place the opinion discusses expert testimony is a separate damages ruling, where it actually restricts an expert because his opinion rested on inadmissible hearsay. Attributing an expert-testimony rule to the interpretive holding overstates what the case decided, so the claim is partial rather than fully supported.

p. — Trident Center v. Connecticut General Life Insurance Co., 847 F.2d 564 (9th Cir. 1988) — Inverts the holding
What the brief claims:

In Trident Center v. Connecticut General Life Insurance Co., 847 F.2d 564 (9th Cir. 1988), the Ninth Circuit, applying California law, reaffirmed that extrinsic evidence (including expert testimony) is admissible to show a term's meaning in the relevant industry, even if the contract appears unambiguous.

Actual language in opinion:

Under traditional contract principles, extrinsic evidence is inadmissible to interpret, vary or add to the terms of an unambiguous integrated written instrument.... Pacific Gas casts a long shadow of uncertainty over all transactions negotiated and executed under the law of California.... While we have our doubts about the wisdom of Pacific Gas, we have no difficulty understanding its meaning.... It may not be a wise rule we are applying, but it is a rule that binds us.

The brief recasts a reluctant, openly critical application of California's parol evidence rule as an affirmative endorsement, which inverts the opinion's posture. Judge Kozinski's opinion holds that under Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. G. W. Thomas Drayage & Rigging Co., a California court must admit extrinsic evidence even where contract language is facially unambiguous, and on that basis reverses and remands. But the court does not "reaffirm" that rule in the approving sense the brief implies; it applies it grudgingly under Erie as binding state law it considers unwise, devoting most of the opinion to criticizing Pacific Gas and inviting the California Supreme Court to reconsider it.

The brief also adds specifics the opinion never contains. The admissibility rule discussed in Trident concerns extrinsic evidence of the parties' actual intent to show that contract language is reasonably susceptible to a proffered meaning; the opinion says nothing about "expert testimony" or about showing "a term's meaning in the relevant industry" (trade-usage or custom-and-usage evidence). Those phrases attribute a principle to the case that does not appear in it. Because the proposition both misstates the court's stance and supplies a holding the opinion does not contain, it is unsupported.

p. — British Motor Car Distributors, Ltd. v. New Motor Vehicle Board, 194 Cal. App. 3d 81 (1987) — Case on unrelated subject
What the brief claims:

In British Motor Car Distributors, Ltd. v. New Motor Vehicle Board, 194 Cal. App. 3d 81 (1987), the court allowed expert testimony to explain the meaning of a term as understood in the automobile industry, emphasizing that industry usage can inform contract interpretation.

British Motor Car Distributors, Ltd. v. New Motor Vehicle Board is a California administrative-mandamus case about whether an automobile distributor lawfully terminated a dealer's franchise under Vehicle Code section 3060. It does not address the admission of expert testimony, industry usage, or the role of trade custom in contract interpretation.

The opinion resolves three issues: the constitutionality of the New Motor Vehicle Board's composition (dealer-member participation and due process), the proper standard of review for the administrative decision (substantial evidence versus independent judgment), and whether the franchise was 'terminated' rather than allowed to lapse. On the termination point the court did discuss implied contract extension from the parties' conduct (Civ. Code sections 1619, 1621), but it never received or discussed expert testimony, never mentions automobile-industry usage as an interpretive aid, and announces no principle that industry custom informs contract meaning. The proposition attributes a specific evidentiary holding to the case that simply does not appear in it.

Methodology & Limitations

  • Quote verification is near-deterministic but limited by the quality of CourtListener's text. Minor discrepancies may reflect OCR artifacts in the source database rather than errors in the brief.
  • Propositional support assessment is an AI judgment. It reflects Claude's reading of the opinion text and should be verified by counsel. Reasonable lawyers may disagree about whether a case "supports" a given proposition.
  • This report checks whether cited cases say what the brief claims they say. It does not assess the overall legal merit of the brief's arguments or whether the cited cases are the best authorities available.
  • Cases marked "unable to verify" should be checked through other sources (Westlaw, Lexis, etc.).
